# Coffee Futures

## Definition

Coffee futures trade in 2 primary contracts: Arabica (ICE US, ticker KC, priced in USD cents/lb) and Robusta (ICE Europe, ticker RC, priced in USD/tonne). Arabica is the higher quality, higher priced variety used in specialty and premium blends. Robusta is cheaper, higher caffeine, used in instant coffee and as a blending component. Brazil dominates Arabica production, Vietnam dominates Robusta.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Coffee is the second most traded commodity by volume after crude oil in some measures. It has extreme weather sensitivity (frost in Brazil can move prices 20%+ in a single session), making it a prime example of [[Convenience Yield]] dynamics and supply shock trading. The Arabica/Robusta spread is one of the most actively traded [[Relative Value Trade|relative value trades]] in soft commodities. Coffee is also a natural laboratory for understanding how [[Seasonality]], weather risk, and inventory cycles interact.

## Fundamentals

### Supply drivers
- Brazil: approximately 35 to 40% of global production. Biennial cycle (on year / off year alternation)
- Vietnam: approximately 18% of production, nearly all Robusta
- Colombia, Honduras, Ethiopia, Uganda: secondary producers
- Frost and drought in Brazil (Jun to Aug): the single biggest price risk factor
- Berry borer beetle and coffee leaf rust: ongoing supply threats
- Brazilian Real (BRL): weaker BRL incentivizes Brazilian farmers to sell, increasing supply

### Demand drivers
- Global consumption approximately 170 million bags/year, growing 2 to 3% annually
- EU is the largest consuming region, followed by US, Japan, Brazil
- Specialty coffee growth driving Arabica premium
- Price elasticity of demand is low in the short term (people need their coffee)

### Seasonality
Brazilian Arabica harvest runs Apr to Sep (main). Vietnamese Robusta harvest runs Oct to Mar. Frost risk window in Brazil is Jun to Aug (Brazilian winter). Prices tend to build a weather premium into Jun/Jul and release it once the frost window passes without incident.

## Key data and reports

- USDA biannual coffee report: global supply/demand estimates
- ICO (International Coffee Organization): monthly export data
- GCA (Green Coffee Association): US warehouse stocks, monthly
- ICE certified stocks: deliverable inventory
- Brazilian crop agency (CONAB): annual production estimates
- CFTC COT report: managed money positioning in KC
- Brazil weather forecasts: SOMAR Meteorologia

## Curve structure

Arabica frequently shifts between [[Contango]] and [[Backwardation]] depending on certified stock levels and harvest expectations. During Brazilian frost scares, the curve can invert violently into backwardation as physical buyers scramble for available supply. Between harvests, mild contango is typical if inventories are adequate.

## Key spreads and relative value

- **[[Arabica-Robusta Spread|Arabica/Robusta spread]]:** the core relative value trade in coffee. Widens when Arabica supply tightens or specialty demand grows. Narrows when roasters substitute Robusta into blends due to high Arabica prices.
- **Coffee calendar spreads:** front vs deferred, particularly around Brazilian frost season.
- **Coffee/Cocoa ratio:** both tropical softs, correlated supply risks, but different geographic concentration.
- **Coffee/Sugar ratio:** Brazilian farmers can switch land between coffee and sugarcane, creating a long run supply substitution linkage.

## Important relationships

- Strong inverse correlation with BRL (weaker Real = more Brazilian selling = lower USD price, all else equal)
- Weather correlation: frost and drought in Minas Gerais (Brazil's largest coffee state) dominate price action
- Positive correlation with [[Cocoa Futures|cocoa]] and [[Sugar Futures|sugar]] during broad soft commodity moves
- ICE certified stocks inversely correlated with price (low stocks = high convenience yield = backwardation)
