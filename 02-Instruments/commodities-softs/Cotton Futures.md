# Cotton Futures

## Definition
Cotton futures are exchange traded contracts for the physical delivery of cotton lint, with the primary benchmark being the ICE Cotton No. 2 contract traded in New York. The contract represents 50,000 lbs (approximately 100 bales) of strict low middling (SLM) grade cotton, priced in cents per pound, with delivery to licensed warehouses in the US. Cotton is unique among agricultural commodities because its demand is driven primarily by the global textile industry, making it sensitive to GDP growth, consumer spending, and fashion cycles rather than food consumption. The United States, India, China, Brazil, and Pakistan are the largest producers, while China is the world's largest consumer by a wide margin. China's state reserve stockpile policy has historically dominated price dynamics, with the government accumulating or releasing tens of millions of bales to manage domestic market stability. Cotton prices exhibit high volatility, with annual trading ranges frequently exceeding 30% of the year's average price.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
Cotton serves as a useful barometer of global manufacturing and consumer demand, providing a cross reference for macro traders monitoring economic cycles and emerging market health. The cotton/polyester substitution dynamic means that sustained cotton price rallies above $0.90 to $1.00/lb accelerate textile mills' switching to synthetic fibers, creating a soft price ceiling driven by demand elasticity. US cotton exports are a significant component of the agricultural trade balance, and Chinese import demand directly influences [[USD/CNY]] flows and trade policy discussions. For physical commodity trading firms, cotton basis trading between origins (US, Brazilian, Indian, West African) and the ICE benchmark provides substantial arbitrage and logistics optimization opportunities.

## Concrete example
**Success case:** In August 2010, severe flooding in Pakistan destroyed approximately 20% of the country's cotton crop, while China's state reserve was already depleted from years of drawdown. A trader went long December 2010 cotton at $0.85/lb. By March 2011, cotton reached $2.27/lb (the highest level since the US Civil War era), yielding a gain of $1.42/lb or $71,000 per contract. The global [[Stocks to Use Ratio]] fell to 37%, the lowest in over 15 years.

**Failure case:** In May 2011, a trader bought July cotton at $1.50/lb expecting the historic rally to continue. China began releasing massive state reserves, and demand destruction from extreme prices accelerated substitution toward polyester. Cotton crashed to $0.88/lb by October, a loss of $0.62/lb or $31,000 per contract. The lesson: policy driven supply releases combined with demand elasticity at extreme price levels can reverse parabolic moves with devastating speed.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **Contract size:** 50,000 lbs (approximately 100 bales of ~480 lbs each)
- **Tick size:** 0.01 cents/lb = $5.00 per tick
- **Daily price limit:** 4 cents/lb ($2,000 per contract), expandable to 5 cents then 7 cents on consecutive limit days
- **Cotlook A Index:** The global physical benchmark, calculated as the average of the 5 cheapest origin offerings CIF Far East. ICE futures typically trade at a variable discount to the A Index.
- **Polyester parity:** When cotton exceeds approximately $0.90 to $1.00/lb, textile mills substitute polyester (derived from crude oil), creating price resistance
- **[[Stocks to Use Ratio]] thresholds (global cotton):** below 40% = tight; 40 to 55% = balanced; above 55% = surplus
- **China state reserve:** At its peak in 2014, China held over 60 million bales, representing roughly 1 full year of Chinese consumption

## Related concepts (learn next)
- [[Stocks to Use Ratio]] for cotton drives the long term price cycle between tight and surplus conditions.
- [[Weather Premium]] applies during the US growing season (June to August) when drought or excessive rain threatens yields in Texas and the Delta region.
- [[WASDE]] includes monthly cotton balance sheet estimates that move futures prices at the global and country level.
- [[COT Positioning]] in cotton often reaches extremes that precede major trend reversals.
- [[Implied Volatility]] in cotton options is typically elevated relative to grains, reflecting the commodity's boom/bust character.
- [[Basis Risk]] is significant in cotton due to quality differentials among grades, staple lengths, and geographic origins.
- [[Contango Roll Cost]] in cotton can erode returns during surplus periods when abundant warehouse stocks suppress nearby premiums.
- [[Spread Trade]] in cotton includes inter crop calendar spreads and cotton versus polyester relative value expressions.

## Common misconceptions
- **"Cotton is purely weather driven."** Demand cycles linked to GDP growth and Chinese stockpile policy are equally important price drivers. The 2011 to 2014 bear market was driven largely by China's massive reserve accumulation program suppressing import demand, not by favorable weather.
- **"US cotton prices reflect global fundamentals."** ICE cotton is a US delivery contract with specific grade and location requirements. Indian and Chinese domestic cotton prices can diverge significantly due to local policies, import tariffs, and quality differences.
- **"Cotton demand is price inelastic."** Above $1.00/lb, textile mills aggressively substitute polyester and blended fabrics. This substitution elasticity provides a demonstrable ceiling on sustained cotton price rallies and has been observed in every major price spike.
