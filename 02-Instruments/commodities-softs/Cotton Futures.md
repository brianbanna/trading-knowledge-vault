# Cotton Futures

## Definition
Cotton futures are exchange traded contracts for physical delivery of cotton lint. The primary benchmark is the ICE Cotton No. 2 contract in New York: 50,000 lbs (~100 bales) of strict low middling (SLM) grade, priced in cents per pound, deliverable to licensed US warehouses. Cotton is unique among agri commodities because demand is driven by the global textile industry, making it sensitive to GDP growth, consumer spending, and fashion cycles rather than food consumption. The US, India, China, Brazil, and Pakistan are the largest producers; China is the largest consumer by a wide margin. China's state reserve policy has historically dominated price dynamics, accumulating or releasing tens of millions of bales to manage domestic stability. Annual trading ranges frequently exceed 30% of the year's average price.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
Cotton is a useful barometer of global manufacturing and consumer demand, a cross reference for macro traders watching economic cycles and emerging market health. The cotton/polyester substitution means sustained rallies above $0.90 to $1.00/lb accelerate textile mill switching to synthetic fibers, creating a soft demand driven price ceiling. US cotton exports are a significant component of the agri trade balance, and Chinese import demand directly influences [[USD/CNY]] flows and trade policy. For physical commodity firms, cotton basis trading between origins (US, Brazilian, Indian, West African) and the ICE benchmark generates substantial arbitrage and logistics optimization.

## Concrete example
**Concrete:** August 2010. Severe flooding in Pakistan destroyed ~20% of the country's cotton crop while China's state reserve was already depleted from years of drawdown. Trader goes long December 2010 cotton at $0.85/lb. By March 2011, cotton reaches $2.27/lb (the highest since the US Civil War era), gain of $1.42/lb or $71,000 per contract. The global [[Stocks to Use Ratio]] falls to 37%, the lowest in over 15 years. In May 2011, a follow on trader buys July at $1.50/lb expecting more upside. China begins releasing massive state reserves and demand destruction accelerates polyester substitution. Cotton crashes to $0.88/lb by October: -$0.62/lb, $31,000 loss per contract.

**Simplified:** Cotton supply shocks (floods, droughts) plus low inventories produce explosive rallies. Above $1.00/lb, mills start swapping cotton for polyester. Government stockpile releases can crush prices overnight. Boom then bust.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **Contract size:** 50,000 lbs (~100 bales of ~480 lbs each)
- **Tick size:** 0.01 cents/lb = $5.00 per tick
- **Daily price limit:** 4 cents/lb ($2,000 per contract), expandable to 5 then 7 cents on consecutive limit days
- **Cotlook A Index:** the global physical benchmark, average of the 5 cheapest origin offerings CIF Far East. ICE futures trade at a variable discount.
- **Polyester parity:** cotton above $0.90 to $1.00/lb pulls mills toward polyester (crude oil derived), creating price resistance.
- **[[Stocks to Use Ratio]] thresholds (global cotton):** below 40% = tight; 40 to 55% = balanced; above 55% = surplus.
- **China state reserve:** at peak in 2014, China held over 60 million bales, roughly 1 full year of Chinese consumption.

## Related concepts (learn next)
- [[Stocks to Use Ratio]] drives the long term cotton price cycle between tight and surplus.
- [[Weather Premium]] applies during the US growing season (June to August) when drought or excessive rain threatens yields in Texas and the Delta region.
- [[WASDE]] includes monthly cotton balance sheet estimates that move futures at global and country level.
- [[COT Positioning]] in cotton reaches extremes that precede major trend reversals.
- [[Implied Volatility]] in cotton options runs high vs grains, reflecting the boom/bust character.
- [[Basis Risk]] is significant in cotton due to grade, staple length, and origin differentials.
- [[Contango Roll Cost]] in cotton erodes returns during surplus periods when abundant warehouse stocks suppress nearby premiums.
- [[Spread Trade]] in cotton includes inter crop calendar spreads and cotton vs polyester relative value expressions.

## Common misconceptions
- **"Cotton is purely weather driven."** GDP growth and Chinese stockpile policy are equally important. The 2011 to 2014 bear market was driven largely by China's reserve accumulation suppressing import demand, not by favorable weather.
- **"US cotton prices reflect global fundamentals."** ICE cotton is a US delivery contract with specific grade and location requirements. Indian and Chinese domestic cotton prices diverge significantly due to local policies, tariffs, and quality differences.
- **"Cotton demand is price inelastic."** Above $1.00/lb, mills aggressively substitute polyester and blended fabrics. Substitution elasticity has provided a demonstrable ceiling on every major price spike.
