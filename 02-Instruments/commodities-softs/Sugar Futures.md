# Sugar Futures

## Definition
Sugar futures are exchange traded contracts for physical delivery of raw or white (refined) sugar. Primary benchmarks: ICE No. 11 (raw cane sugar, New York) and ICE No. 5 (white sugar, London). No. 11 represents 112,000 lbs of raw cane sugar, priced in cents per pound, deliverable at seller designated ports in producing countries. Sugar is unique because it has a direct substitute use as ethanol feedstock, particularly in Brazil, creating a price floor linked to sugar/ethanol parity. Brazil is the world's largest producer and exporter (~20 to 25% of global production), making it the swing producer determining global surplus or deficit. India, Thailand, and the EU are other major players, each with distinct government intervention (subsidies, export quotas, minimum price guarantees). Sugar markets show strong multi year cycles driven by sugarcane planting and ratoon cycles of 5 to 6 years.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
Sugar is one of the most actively traded softs, with deep liquidity and high annualized volatility (25 to 35%), attractive to hedgers and speculators alike. The sugar/ethanol parity in Brazil links sugar prices directly to energy markets, adding a cross commodity dimension that connects sugar to [[Crude Oil]] pricing. The Brazilian real ([[BRL/USD]]) is strongly correlated with sugar: a weaker real lowers Brazil's dollar denominated production cost, incentivizing exports and pressuring global prices. Indian government policy (export subsidies, domestic stock limits) can add 2 to 5 cents/lb of policy driven volatility in a single season.

## Concrete example
**Concrete:** October 2020. Raw sugar (No. 11) trades at $0.13/lb. Trader recognizes Brazilian mills are maximizing ethanol as crude oil prices recover post COVID, diverting cane from sugar. She goes long March 2021 No. 11 at $0.14/lb. By February 2021, sugar reaches $0.17/lb: +$0.03/lb or $3,360 per contract (112,000 lbs). Sugar/ethanol parity has shifted decisively to favor ethanol, tightening sugar supply globally. In 2017, the reverse case: trader buys October sugar at $0.15/lb expecting Indian monsoon disruption to cut production. India instead produces a record 32 million tons in 2017/18 and the government subsidizes exports to dispose of surplus. Sugar collapses to $0.11/lb by September: $4,480 loss per contract.

**Simplified:** Brazilian mills can produce sugar or ethanol from the same cane. High crude oil prices pull cane into ethanol and lift sugar. Indian government policy can flood or tighten the global market on its own. Trade the Brazilian energy link and watch Indian policy headlines.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **Contract size (No. 11):** 112,000 lbs of raw sugar
- **Tick size:** 0.01 cents/lb = $11.20 per tick
- **Sugar/ethanol parity (Brazil):** approximate sugar floor = (Hydrous ethanol price in BRL/liter x conversion factor) / BRL/USD. When raw sugar falls below ~$0.12/lb equivalent, mills shift cane to ethanol.
- **White premium = No. 5 (white) price - No. 11 (raw) price.** Reflects refining margin, typically $60 to $100/metric ton.
- **Global balance = Production - Consumption = Surplus or Deficit.** Key sources: USDA FAS, International Sugar Organization (ISO).
- **Seasonal pattern:** prices strengthen Q4 to Q1 (Northern Hemisphere off crop) and weaken Q2 to Q3 (Brazil center south harvest).

## Related concepts (learn next)
- [[Weather Premium]] is relevant when drought threatens Brazil's center south cane or the Indian monsoon disrupts planting.
- [[Contango Roll Cost]] can be significant in sugar during multi year surplus periods when warehouse stocks accumulate.
- [[Backwardation Roll Gain]] occurs in deficit periods when nearby sugar is physically scarce.
- [[COT Positioning]] in sugar frequently reaches extremes that offer contrarian signals.
- [[Implied Volatility]] in sugar options runs higher than in grains due to overlapping policy and weather uncertainty.
- [[Basis Risk]] exists between the No. 11 benchmark and domestic physical sugar prices in producing countries.
- [[Stocks to Use Ratio]] for sugar globally determines whether the market is in structural surplus or deficit.
- [[Crush Spread]] is conceptually analogous: the white premium serves a similar role to processing margins in oilseeds.
- [[Sugar-Ethanol Spread]] is the production switching option that sets how much Brazilian cane becomes exportable sugar, the single most mobile variable in the global supply balance.

## Common misconceptions
- **"Sugar is just a food commodity."** Brazil's flex fuel vehicle fleet means 40 to 50% of Brazilian cane can be directed to ethanol rather than sugar. Sugar is as much an energy commodity as a food one, and crude oil prices directly affect sugar supply.
- **"Low prices always recover quickly."** Sugar ran a sustained bear market from $0.36/lb in early 2011 to $0.10/lb in late 2015, over 4 years. Surplus cycles driven by Indian and Thai overproduction persist across multiple seasons.
- **"The No. 11 price reflects all sugar."** No. 11 is raw cane sugar delivered at origin. Beet sugar (EU), refined white sugar (No. 5), and US domestic sugar (No. 16) diverge significantly from the global No. 11 benchmark due to tariffs, quotas, and quality.
