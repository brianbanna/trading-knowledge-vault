# Sugar Futures

## Definition
Sugar futures are exchange traded contracts for the physical delivery of raw or white (refined) sugar, with the primary benchmarks being the ICE No. 11 (raw sugar, traded in New York) and ICE No. 5 (white sugar, traded in London). The No. 11 contract represents 112,000 lbs of raw cane sugar, priced in cents per pound, with delivery at ports designated by the seller in producing countries. Sugar is unique among commodities because it has a direct substitute use as ethanol feedstock, particularly in Brazil, creating a price floor linked to the sugar/ethanol parity calculation. Brazil is the world's largest producer and exporter, accounting for roughly 20 to 25% of global production and making it the swing producer that determines global surplus or deficit. India, Thailand, and the EU are other major players, each with distinct government intervention policies including subsidies, export quotas, and minimum price guarantees. Sugar markets exhibit strong multi year cycles driven by sugarcane planting and ratoon cycles of 5 to 6 years from initial planting to replanting.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
Sugar is one of the most actively traded soft commodities, with deep liquidity and high annualized volatility (typically 25 to 35%), making it attractive for both hedgers and speculative traders. The sugar/ethanol parity in Brazil creates a direct link between sugar prices and energy markets, adding a cross commodity dimension that connects sugar to [[Crude Oil]] pricing. The Brazilian real ([[BRL/USD]]) is strongly correlated with sugar prices: a weaker real lowers Brazil's dollar denominated cost of production, incentivizing exports and pressuring global sugar prices. Indian government policy (export subsidies, domestic stock limits) can add 2 to 5 cents/lb of policy driven volatility in a single season.

## Concrete example
**Success case:** In October 2020, raw sugar (No. 11) traded at $0.13/lb. A trader recognized that Brazilian mills were maximizing ethanol production as crude oil prices recovered post COVID, diverting cane away from sugar. They went long March 2021 No. 11 at $0.14/lb. By February 2021, sugar reached $0.17/lb, a gain of $0.03/lb or $3,360 per contract (112,000 lbs). The sugar/ethanol parity had shifted decisively to favor ethanol, tightening sugar supply globally.

**Failure case:** In 2017, a trader bought October sugar at $0.15/lb expecting India's monsoon disruptions to cut production. Instead, India produced a record 32 million metric tons in the 2017/18 season, and the government subsidized exports to dispose of surplus. Sugar collapsed to $0.11/lb by September, a loss of $0.04/lb or $4,480 per contract. The lesson: Indian policy can flood the global market regardless of other bullish factors elsewhere.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **Contract size (No. 11):** 112,000 lbs of raw sugar
- **Tick size:** 0.01 cents/lb = $11.20 per tick
- **Sugar/ethanol parity (Brazil):** Approximate sugar price floor = (Hydrous ethanol price in BRL/liter x conversion factor) / BRL/USD exchange rate. When raw sugar falls below roughly $0.12/lb equivalent, mills shift cane allocation toward ethanol.
- **White premium = No. 5 (white) price - No. 11 (raw) price.** Reflects the refining margin, typically $60 to $100/metric ton.
- **Global balance = Production - Consumption = Surplus or Deficit.** Key sources: USDA Foreign Agricultural Service, International Sugar Organization (ISO).
- **Seasonal pattern:** Prices tend to strengthen in Q4 to Q1 (Northern Hemisphere off crop period) and weaken in Q2 to Q3 (Brazil center south harvest).

## Related concepts (learn next)
- [[Weather Premium]] is relevant when drought threatens Brazil's center south cane crop or the Indian monsoon disrupts planting.
- [[Contango Roll Cost]] can be significant in sugar during multi year surplus periods when warehouse stocks accumulate.
- [[Backwardation Roll Gain]] occurs in deficit periods when nearby sugar is physically scarce.
- [[COT Positioning]] in sugar frequently reaches extremes that offer contrarian trading signals.
- [[Implied Volatility]] in sugar options tends to run higher than in grains due to overlapping policy and weather uncertainty.
- [[Basis Risk]] exists between the No. 11 benchmark and domestic physical sugar prices in producing countries.
- [[Stocks to Use Ratio]] for sugar globally determines whether the market is in structural surplus or deficit.
- [[Crush Spread]] is conceptually analogous: the white premium in sugar serves a similar role to processing margins in oilseeds.

## Common misconceptions
- **"Sugar is just a food commodity."** Brazil's flex fuel vehicle fleet means 40 to 50% of Brazilian cane output can be directed to ethanol rather than sugar. Sugar is as much an energy commodity as a food one, and crude oil prices directly affect sugar supply.
- **"Low prices will always recover quickly."** Sugar experienced a sustained bear market from $0.36/lb in early 2011 to $0.10/lb in late 2015, lasting over 4 years. Surplus cycles driven by Indian and Thai overproduction can persist across multiple seasons.
- **"The No. 11 price reflects all sugar."** The No. 11 is raw cane sugar delivered at origin. Beet sugar (EU), refined white sugar (No. 5), and US domestic sugar (No. 16 contract) can all diverge significantly from the global No. 11 benchmark due to tariffs, quotas, and quality differences.
