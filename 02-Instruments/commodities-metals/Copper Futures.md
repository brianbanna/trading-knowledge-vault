# Copper Futures

## Definition

Copper is the most actively traded base metal, often called "Dr. Copper" because its price is considered a leading indicator of global economic health. It trades primarily on COMEX (CME Group) and the [[LME]] (London Metal Exchange). Copper's unique position at the intersection of construction, manufacturing, electrical infrastructure, and the energy transition makes it one of the most fundamentally driven commodity markets.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Copper is a macro barometer. When copper rallies, it typically signals global industrial expansion, particularly in China which consumes approximately 50% of global refined copper. For a commodities trader, copper is a core position in any cross commodity portfolio and a natural hedge against or expression of global growth views. It also links directly to [[FX Carry Trade|FX]] through commodity currency correlations (AUD, CLP, PEN, ZMW).

The copper market is also a key [[Relative Value Trade]] market: copper vs aluminum, copper vs iron ore, and copper calendar spreads are all actively traded by institutional desks.

## Fundamentals

### Supply drivers
- Mine production: Chile (27%), Peru (10%), DRC (10%), China (8%) are top producers
- Smelter capacity and treatment/refining charges (TC/RCs)
- Scrap supply (approximately 30% of total copper supply is recycled)
- Mine disruptions: strikes, weather, political instability in Latin America and Africa
- Capex cycles: 7 to 10 year lag from discovery to production

### Demand drivers
- Construction and building (28% of demand): wiring, plumbing, roofing
- Electrical and electronic products (30%): power cables, transformers, motors
- Transportation (12%): EVs use 3 to 4x more copper than ICE vehicles
- Industrial machinery (12%)
- China accounts for approximately 50% of global refined demand
- Energy transition: solar, wind, grid upgrades, EV charging infrastructure

### Seasonality
Moderate seasonal pattern. Chinese construction and manufacturing activity typically peaks in Q1 and Q3 (before Lunar New Year restocking, and post summer resumption). LME warehouse stocks tend to build in Q4 as demand softens.

## Key data and reports

- ICSG (International Copper Study Group): monthly supply/demand balance
- Chinese copper imports: monthly customs data, critical demand signal
- LME warehouse stocks: daily, shows available deliverable inventory
- COMEX warehouse stocks: weekly
- Chilean copper production: Cochilco monthly data
- PMI data (global): leading indicator for copper demand
- China property data: housing starts, construction activity

## Curve structure

Copper typically trades in mild [[Contango]] reflecting financing and storage costs. Shifts to [[Backwardation]] during supply squeezes or when LME warehouse stocks draw down sharply. The LME has a unique prompt date structure (daily dates rather than monthly) which creates more granular curve trading opportunities than COMEX.

## Key spreads and relative value

- **Copper/Gold ratio:** macro indicator for growth vs safety sentiment. Rising ratio = risk on.
- **Copper/Aluminum spread:** relative industrial demand. Copper is more electrically focused, aluminum more construction and packaging.
- **COMEX/LME arb:** pricing differential between US and international copper, reflecting regional supply/demand and logistics.
- **Copper/Iron ore ratio:** construction demand indicator. Both driven by China but different supply dynamics.
- **Calendar spreads:** LME tom/next and cash/3 month spreads signal physical tightness.

## Important relationships

- Strong positive correlation with Chinese PMI and industrial production
- Positive correlation with [[AUD/USD|AUD]] (Australia is a major copper exporter)
- Inverse correlation with [[DXY]] (USD strength) in risk on/off regimes
- Correlated with global equity markets as a growth proxy
- Lead/lag relationship with [[Iron Ore Futures|iron ore]] on China property cycle
