# Tin Futures

## Definition

Tin is the smallest and most illiquid of the six base metals traded on the [[LME]]. Its primary use is in solder (approximately 50% of demand), which is essential for connecting electronic components in circuit boards, making tin a proxy for global electronics manufacturing. Other uses include tinplate (food cans), chemicals, and alloys. Global mine production is approximately 300,000 tonnes per year, highly concentrated in China (~30%), Indonesia (~25%), and Myanmar (~10%). The combination of small market size, concentrated supply, and inelastic demand makes tin one of the most volatile base metals, capable of sharp price moves on relatively small supply disruptions.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Tin's link to electronics and semiconductor manufacturing makes it a niche but important indicator for tech cycle traders. Supply disruptions in Indonesia (export restrictions, artisanal mining regulations) or Myanmar (conflict related) can move tin prices 10 to 20% in weeks due to the thin market. For base metals traders, tin offers diversification because its demand drivers (electronics) differ from the construction focused metals (copper, zinc, aluminum). However, low liquidity means wider [[Bid-Ask Spread]]s and greater [[Slippage]] risk on larger positions.

## Concrete example

Indonesia announces a temporary ban on tin ingot exports to conduct mining license audits. Tin is at $25,000/tonne. A trader goes long expecting the export disruption to tighten the market. In 4 weeks, with Indonesian exports halted, LME tin stocks fall and the price rallies to $30,000/tonne, earning $5,000/tonne. If the ban had been quickly reversed or other producers ramped up to fill the gap, tin might have only briefly spiked before falling back to $24,000, resulting in a modest loss.

| Field | Value |
|---|---|

| Ticker | SN |
| Contract size | 5 metric tonnes |
| Quotation | USD per tonne |

**Demand breakdown:**
- Solder: ~50%
- Tinplate: ~15%
- Chemicals: ~15%
- Other (alloys, glass, etc.): ~20%

**Supply concentration risk:** Top 3 producers (China, Indonesia, Myanmar) account for ~65% of global mine output.

## Common misconceptions

**"Tin is irrelevant because the market is so small."** Tin is essential for electronics manufacturing. No solder means no circuit boards, no smartphones, no servers. Small market, critical application.

**"Tin volatility means big opportunities."** The thin market also means wide spreads and slippage. A $5,000/tonne move looks great until you realize execution costs on larger positions can be $200 to $500/tonne.

**"Lead free solder has replaced tin."** Lead free solder actually uses MORE tin, not less. The transition to lead free electronics has been structurally bullish for tin demand.
