# Tin Futures

## Definition

Tin is the smallest and most illiquid of the 6 base metals traded on the [[LME]]. Primary use is solder (~50% of demand), essential for connecting electronic components in circuit boards, making tin a proxy for global electronics manufacturing. Other uses: tinplate (food cans), chemicals, alloys. Global mine production is ~300,000 tonnes/year, concentrated in China (~30%), Indonesia (~25%), and Myanmar (~10%). Small market size + concentrated supply + inelastic demand make tin one of the most volatile base metals, capable of sharp price moves on relatively small supply disruptions.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Tin's link to electronics and semiconductors makes it a niche but important indicator for tech cycle traders. Supply disruptions in Indonesia (export restrictions, mining regulations) or Myanmar (conflict related) move tin prices 10 to 20% in weeks due to the thin market. For base metals traders, tin offers diversification because its demand driver (electronics) differs from the construction focused metals (copper, zinc, aluminum). Low liquidity means wider [[Bid-Ask Spread]]s and greater [[Slippage]] risk on larger positions.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** Indonesia announces a temporary tin ingot export ban to conduct mining license audits. Tin at $25,000/tonne. Trader goes long, expecting export disruption to tighten the market. In 4 weeks, with Indonesian exports halted, LME tin stocks fall and price rallies to $30,000: +$5,000/tonne. If the ban had been quickly reversed or other producers ramped to fill the gap, tin might have briefly spiked before falling back to $24,000, a modest loss.

**Simplified:** Tin is a tiny market. When a major producer (Indonesia, Myanmar) cuts exports, there is no buffer and price spikes hard. Small disruptions create big moves. Liquidity is poor, so execution costs eat returns.

|---|---|

| Ticker | SN |
| Contract size | 5 metric tonnes |

**Demand breakdown:**
- Solder: ~50%
- Tinplate: ~15%
- Chemicals: ~15%
- Other (alloys, glass, etc.): ~20%

**Supply concentration risk:** Top 3 producers (China, Indonesia, Myanmar) account for ~65% of global mine output.

## Common misconceptions

**"Tin is irrelevant because the market is small."** Tin is essential for electronics manufacturing. No solder means no circuit boards, no smartphones, no servers. Small market, critical application.

**"Tin volatility means big opportunities."** Thin market also means wide spreads and slippage. A $5,000/tonne move looks great until execution costs on larger positions run $200 to $500/tonne.

**"Lead free solder has replaced tin."** Lead free solder actually uses MORE tin, not less. The transition to lead free electronics has been structurally bullish for tin demand.
