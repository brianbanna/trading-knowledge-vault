# Aluminum Futures

## Definition

Aluminum is the most traded base metal by volume on the [[LME]] and the most widely used non ferrous metal in the world. It is prized for its light weight, corrosion resistance, and conductivity, with primary applications in transport (automotive, aerospace), packaging (beverage cans, foil), construction (facades, window frames), and electrical (power cables). Aluminum production is extremely energy intensive: smelting requires approximately 14,000 kWh per tonne, making electricity the single largest cost input (30 to 40% of production cost). China dominates global production at approximately 60% of output, and the marginal cost of production is largely determined by Chinese electricity prices and environmental policy.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Aluminum is a proxy for global energy and industrial policy. Because production costs are dominated by electricity, aluminum prices are sensitive to energy price shocks, carbon pricing, and Chinese power market reforms. When Chinese authorities curtail smelting to meet emissions targets (as in 2021), aluminum supply contracts sharply. The metal also has a significant scrap supply (recycled aluminum requires only 5% of the energy of primary production), creating a scrap/primary price ratio that acts as a soft ceiling. For cross asset traders, aluminum correlates with Chinese industrial production, European energy costs, and AUD.

## Concrete example

A trader expects European natural gas prices (TTF) to spike in Q4 2025, raising European smelter costs above $2,800/tonne. With LME aluminum at $2,400, European smelters are at risk of curtailing production. She goes long LME aluminum at $2,400. If TTF spikes and 1 million tonnes of European capacity is curtailed, LME aluminum could rally to $2,700, earning $300/tonne. If TTF falls instead and smelters keep running, the supply remains ample and aluminum drops to $2,250, losing $150/tonne.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**LME contract specifications:**
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Exchange | LME |
| Ticker | AH |
| Contract size | 25 metric tonnes |
| Quotation | USD per tonne |
| Settlement | Physical delivery (LME warehouse) |
| Prompt dates | Daily to 3 months, monthly to 63 months |

**Production cost breakdown:**
- Electricity: 30 to 40%
- Alumina: 30 to 35%
- Other costs (carbon, labor, overhead): 25 to 35%

**Energy conversion:** ~14,000 kWh per tonne. At $0.05/kWh = $700 energy cost. At $0.10/kWh = $1,400 energy cost.

## Common misconceptions

**"Aluminum is just a base metal like copper."** Aluminum's production economics are fundamentally different, dominated by electricity costs rather than mine output. Trading aluminum requires understanding energy markets.

**"China's 60% market share means Chinese policy is the only driver."** European and Middle Eastern smelter economics, Russian supply disruptions, and Indian demand growth all matter for the global balance.

**"LME aluminum price is what end users pay."** End users pay LME + regional premium + conversion premium. The all in cost can be 15 to 25% above the LME price.
