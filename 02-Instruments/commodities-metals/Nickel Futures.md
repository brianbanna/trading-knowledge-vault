# Nickel Futures

## Definition

Nickel is one of the most volatile base metals, used primarily in stainless steel production (~70% of demand) and increasingly in electric vehicle batteries (nickel sulfate for lithium ion cathodes). It trades on the [[LME]] (6 tonne lots) and [[SHFE]]. A critical distinction in the nickel market is between Class 1 nickel (refined, battery grade, > 99.8% purity, deliverable on LME) and Class 2 nickel (ferronickel and nickel pig iron, used in stainless steel, not LME deliverable). Indonesia dominates global mine production (~50%) and has transformed the market through massive nickel pig iron (NPI) capacity expansion. The 2022 LME nickel short squeeze, which saw prices spike to $100,000/tonne before the exchange cancelled trades, highlighted the market's structural fragility.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Nickel sits at the intersection of two mega trends: stainless steel demand (China construction and infrastructure) and EV battery demand (the energy transition). The Class 1/Class 2 split creates pricing dislocations because LME nickel (Class 1) can trade at a significant premium or discount to the NPI/ferronickel market. Indonesian HPAL (High Pressure Acid Leaching) plants are attempting to bridge this gap by converting laterite ore into battery grade nickel, but the technology is still scaling. For traders, nickel's volatility and the Class 1/Class 2 dynamics create opportunities in both outright and relative value trades.

## Concrete example

A trader expects EV sales in Europe to accelerate, tightening Class 1 nickel supply for battery production. LME nickel is at $16,000/tonne. She goes long, targeting $19,000. If European EV mandates tighten and battery producers scramble for Class 1 supply, LME nickel could rally to $20,000, earning $4,000/tonne. If instead Indonesian HPAL output ramps faster than expected, flooding the Class 1 market, nickel could fall to $14,000, losing $2,000/tonne.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**LME contract specifications:**
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Exchange | LME |
| Ticker | NI |
| Contract size | 6 metric tonnes |
| Quotation | USD per tonne |
| Settlement | Physical delivery (LME warehouse) |

**Nickel classes:**
- Class 1: > 99.8% Ni, LME deliverable (cathode, briquettes, rounds)
- Class 2: ferronickel (20 to 40% Ni), NPI (8 to 15% Ni), not LME deliverable

**Key supply sources:**
- Indonesia: ~50% of mine production (mostly NPI/ferronickel)
- Philippines: ~10%
- Russia (Nornickel): ~6%, Class 1
- New Caledonia, Australia, Canada: smaller shares

## Common misconceptions

**"LME nickel represents the whole nickel market."** LME only prices Class 1 nickel, which is a shrinking share of global production. The majority of nickel produced (NPI, ferronickel) is not LME deliverable.

**"EV demand will make nickel prices go to the moon."** Indonesian NPI capacity has actually crashed nickel prices by creating a Class 2 surplus. Battery demand is growing but so is Indonesian supply.

**"The 2022 squeeze was a one off."** The structural conditions (small LME deliverable supply, concentrated short positions) persist to varying degrees. Nickel remains vulnerable to delivery squeezes.
