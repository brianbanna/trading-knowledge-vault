# Dubai Crude

## Definition

Dubai crude is the primary pricing benchmark for medium sour crude oil flowing from the Middle East to Asia. It is a blend of Dubai and Oman crudes, representing the quality profile typical of Persian Gulf exports: approximately 31 API gravity and 2% sulfur content. Unlike [[Brent Crude]] (which is light sweet and seaborne from the North Sea) or [[WTI Crude Oil]] (which is landlocked and delivered at Cushing), Dubai is priced through an OTC swaps market managed by Platts and trades as a deliverable forward contract on the Dubai Mercantile Exchange (DME). It serves as the reference price for roughly 2/3 of all crude oil sold to Asia, including Saudi Aramco's Official Selling Prices (OSPs).

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

If you trade any crude flowing East of Suez, Dubai is the reference price. Saudi Aramco, Kuwait, Iraq, and UAE all set their Asian export prices as differentials to Dubai/Oman. The [[EFS]] (Exchange of Futures for Swaps) spread links Brent futures to Dubai swaps, making it the bridge between Atlantic Basin and Asian crude pricing. When Asian demand is strong relative to the Atlantic, the Brent/Dubai spread narrows. When Atlantic tightens, the spread widens. Understanding Dubai pricing is essential for anyone trading Middle East to Asia crude flows, refining margins in Asia, or cross basin [[Location Arbitrage]].

## Concrete example

A trader notices that Chinese refinery runs are surging in Q1 2026, pulling more Middle East barrels East. Dubai swaps for March delivery are at $78/bbl while [[Brent Crude]] March futures are at $82/bbl, putting the [[EFS]] at $4/bbl. The trader expects Asian demand to keep tightening, narrowing the EFS. She buys Dubai swaps and sells Brent futures. If the EFS narrows to $2.50, she profits $1.50/bbl on the spread. If instead a surprise OPEC+ output increase floods the market and Asian demand disappoints, the EFS could widen to $5.50, losing $1.50/bbl.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Dubai swap pricing:** Based on Platts' Market on Close (MOC) assessment of partials (25,000 barrel cargoes) traded in the [[Platts Window]].

**EFS (Exchange of Futures for Swaps):** `EFS = Brent Futures Price - Dubai Swap Price`

**OSP formula (Saudi Aramco to Asia):** `Selling Price = Dubai/Oman Average + Aramco Differential`

The Aramco differential is adjusted monthly and is one of the most watched pricing signals in global oil markets.

## Common misconceptions

**"Dubai is just another grade like Brent or WTI."** Dubai is fundamentally different in market structure. It trades as OTC swaps and forwards rather than exchange listed futures (though DME lists an Oman contract). The pricing mechanism relies on Platts assessments, not continuous exchange trading.

**"Dubai only matters if you trade Middle East crude."** Dubai pricing affects any trader exposed to Asian refining margins, Asian fuel oil markets, or commodity currencies linked to Asian demand (AUD, IDR). It is the demand side benchmark for the world's fastest growing oil consuming region.

**"The EFS is stable."** The Brent/Dubai spread can swing significantly based on Atlantic vs Pacific Basin dynamics, seasonal refinery demand shifts, and OPEC allocation decisions. It is actively traded as a relative value position.
