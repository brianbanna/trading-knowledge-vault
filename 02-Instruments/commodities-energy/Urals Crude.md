# Urals Crude

## Definition

Urals is Russia's primary export grade, a medium sour blend at 31 to 32 API and 1.2 to 1.5% sulfur. It blends light sweet Western Siberian crude with heavier sour Volga-Urals crude, exported mainly through Primorsk (Baltic) and Novorossiysk (Black Sea). Historically Urals traded at a $1 to $3 discount to [[Brent Crude]], reflecting quality and freight from Russian ports. Since 2022 sanctions, the discount has run $10 to $30 below Brent, creating a 2 tier global oil market.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Russia produces 10 to 11 million barrels per day, one of the top 3 globally. The Urals/Brent differential is the cleanest gauge of sanction effectiveness and market segmentation. Steep discounts mean Russian oil flows to price sensitive buyers (India, China, Turkey) below market, while European refiners must source alternative medium sour grades at premiums. The differential creates trades in alternative grade spreads, freight (longer hauls to India/China), and European refining margin dislocations.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** Early 2023. Urals at $55/bbl, Brent at $82, a $27 discount. Indian refiner buys a VLCC of Urals. Freight from Baltic to India: $8/bbl (longer route than historical Rotterdam delivery). Delivered cost: $63/bbl vs $82 Brent equivalent. Refiner captures $15 to $19/bbl extra margin vs Brent based crudes. If sanctions tighten (insurance restrictions, price cap enforcement), Urals supply to India risks disruption and the refiner pays up for alternatives. If sanctions loosen, the discount narrows and margin advantage shrinks.

**Simplified:** Russia sells crude at a forced discount because Western buyers cannot purchase it. Asian refiners get cheap oil and pocket the spread. Europe pays up for replacement barrels. The size of the discount tracks how well sanctions are being enforced.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Urals differential:** `Urals Price = Brent Price - Urals Discount`

**G7 price cap (December 2022):** Western shipping, insurance, and financing prohibited for Russian crude sold above $60/bbl. Creates a floor/ceiling dynamic where Urals hovers near the cap.

**Delivered cost to India/China:** `Landed Cost = Urals FOB + Freight + Insurance`

## Common misconceptions

**"Urals is no longer relevant because of sanctions."** Russia still exports 7 to 8 million barrels per day of crude and products. Urals remains one of the most physically traded grades globally, just to different buyers.

**"The price cap effectively controls Urals."** The cap is widely circumvented through shadow fleets, non Western insurance, and intermediary trading. Urals trades above the $60 cap in practice.

**"European refiners easily replaced Urals."** Many European refineries were optimized for Urals quality. Switching to CPC Blend, Johan Sverdrup, or Middle East medium sour required adjustments and higher cost.
