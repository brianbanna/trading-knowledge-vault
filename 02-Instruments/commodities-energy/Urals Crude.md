# Urals Crude

## Definition

Urals crude is Russia's primary export grade, a medium sour blend with approximately 31 to 32 API gravity and 1.2 to 1.5% sulfur content. It is a blend of light sweet Western Siberian crude and heavier, more sour crude from the Volga-Urals region, exported primarily through the Baltic port of Primorsk and the Black Sea port of Novorossiysk. Historically, Urals traded at a discount of $1 to $3 per barrel to [[Brent Crude]], reflecting its lower quality and the cost of shipping from Russian ports. Since the imposition of Western sanctions in 2022, Urals has traded at much steeper discounts ($10 to $30 below Brent), creating a two tier global oil market.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Russia produces approximately 10 to 11 million barrels per day, making it one of the world's top 3 producers. The Urals differential to Brent is a barometer of sanction effectiveness and global crude market segmentation. Steep Urals discounts mean Russian oil flows to price sensitive buyers (India, China, Turkey) at below market rates, while European refiners who historically processed Urals must source alternative medium sour grades at higher prices. For traders, the Urals differential creates opportunities in alternative grade spreads, freight markets (longer haul routes to India/China), and refining margin dislocations in Europe.

## Concrete example

In early 2023, Urals trades at $55/bbl while Brent is at $82/bbl, a $27 discount. An Indian refiner buys a VLCC cargo of Urals at the discounted price. Freight from the Baltic to India costs $8/bbl (longer route than the historical Rotterdam delivery). The effective delivered cost is $63/bbl vs $82 for Brent equivalent quality. The refiner earns an extra $15 to $19/bbl in refining margin compared to processing Brent based crudes. If sanctions enforcement tightens (insurance restrictions, price cap enforcement), Urals supply to India could be disrupted, forcing the refiner to pay up for alternative grades. If sanctions loosen, the discount narrows and the margin advantage shrinks.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Urals differential:** `Urals Price = Brent Price - Urals Discount`

**G7 price cap (December 2022):** Western shipping, insurance, and financing services prohibited for Russian crude sold above $60/bbl. This creates a hard floor/ceiling dynamic where Urals tends to hover near the cap.

**Delivered cost to India/China:** `Landed Cost = Urals FOB + Freight + Insurance`

## Common misconceptions

**"Urals crude is no longer relevant because of sanctions."** Russia still exports 7 to 8 million barrels per day of crude and products. Urals remains one of the most physically traded grades globally, just to different buyers than before.

**"The price cap effectively controls the Urals price."** The cap is widely circumvented through shadow fleets, non Western insurance, and intermediary trading. Urals often trades above the $60 cap in practice.

**"European refiners easily replaced Urals."** Many European refineries were optimized for Urals quality. Switching to alternative grades (CPC Blend, Johan Sverdrup, Middle East medium sour) required adjustments and often came at higher cost.
