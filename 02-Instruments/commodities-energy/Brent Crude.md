# Brent Crude

## Overview

Brent crude is the global benchmark for seaborne crude oil, pricing approximately 2/3 of the world's internationally traded crude. Originally named after the Brent oilfield in the North Sea, the benchmark has evolved from a single field's production into a basket of 5 North Sea crudes (BFOET: Brent, Forties, Oseberg, Ekofisk, Troll). Brent is a light, sweet crude (approximately 38 API gravity, ~0.4% sulfur). It trades on ICE Futures Europe and as a financially settled contract on CME (BZ). The BFOET basket was expanded to include Troll in 2023 and may include WTI Midland delivered into Rotterdam, reflecting Platts' efforts to maintain liquidity as North Sea production declines.

## Fundamentals

### Supply Drivers
- OPEC+ production decisions (Saudi Arabia, Russia, UAE are key swing producers)
- Non OPEC supply growth: US shale, Brazil pre salt, Guyana
- North Sea production decline (BFOET basket redesigned to compensate)
- Geopolitical disruptions: Middle East, Russia, Libya, Nigeria, Venezuela
- Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR) releases

### Demand Drivers
- Global refinery throughput and margins ([[Crack Spread]])
- Chinese and Indian import demand (the marginal barrels)
- Seasonal patterns: driving season (Q2/Q3), winter heating demand
- Jet fuel recovery cycles (post pandemic normalization)
- Petrochemical feedstock demand (naphtha cracking)

### Seasonality
Q2 tends to be strongest (refinery turnaround ends, summer driving ramps). Q4 can see demand dip before winter heating offsets. OPEC meetings (typically Jun and Nov/Dec) create event risk. Turnaround season (Mar to May, Sep to Oct) reduces refinery demand for crude temporarily.

## Curve Structure
Brent oscillates between [[Contango]] and [[Backwardation]] depending on the supply/demand balance. Backwardation is common during supply tightness (OPEC cuts, geopolitical disruptions, low inventories). Contango prevails during oversupply (demand destruction, OPEC price wars, inventory builds). The time spread between the 1st and 6th month Brent futures (M1 to M6) is a widely watched indicator of market tightness. Steep backwardation (>$5 M1 to M6) signals acute tightness. [[Convenience Yield]] dominates the front of the curve during draws.

## Key Spreads and Relative Value
- [[Brent-WTI Spread]]: the most traded inter benchmark crude spread. Reflects Atlantic Basin economics, US export capacity, and relative supply/demand.
- Brent/Dubai spread (EFS, Exchange of Futures for Swaps): East vs West crude pricing. Widening = Atlantic Basin tighter than Asia.
- [[Crack Spread]] (Brent based): Brent vs gasoil and gasoline. Measures European refining margins.
- [[Calendar Spread]]: M1 vs M2, M1 vs M6, Dec/Dec annual spreads. Core relative value trades.
- DFL (Dated to Frontline): spread between Dated Brent and the front month ICE Brent futures. Signals physical market tightness.

## Important Relationships
- Negative [[Correlation]] with USD (DXY): stronger dollar = lower oil in local currency terms for importers, dampening demand
- Positive correlation with [[Commodity Currencies]] (CAD, NOK, RUB)
- Drives [[Crack Spread]] economics for European refiners
- OPEC+ decisions are the single largest short term supply variable
- Linked to [[Geopolitical Risk]] in Middle East and Russia
