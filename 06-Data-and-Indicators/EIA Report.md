# EIA Report

## Definition

The EIA Weekly Petroleum Status Report is published every Wednesday at 10:30 AM Eastern Time by the US Energy Information Administration. It is the highest frequency, most market moving fundamental data release in energy markets. The report covers US crude oil inventories, gasoline inventories, distillate (diesel/heating oil) inventories, refinery utilization rates, crude oil production estimates, imports, and exports. Crude inventories at Cushing, Oklahoma (the delivery point for [[WTI Crude Oil]] futures) are reported separately and are particularly closely watched because Cushing stocks directly affect the WTI front month price and [[Calendar Spread]] shape.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

The EIA report moves oil prices within seconds of release. Surprise builds (inventories higher than expected) are bearish, while surprise draws (inventories lower than expected) are bullish. The consensus estimate is compiled from analyst surveys, and the deviation from consensus drives the immediate price reaction. Crude oil futures, refined product futures, and energy equities all react. The report also provides the most granular view of US supply/demand fundamentals available, making it essential for any oil trader's weekly workflow. The [[API Report]] on Tuesday evening often provides a preview, but the EIA is the official and authoritative source.

## Concrete example

Consensus expects a crude draw of 2 million barrels for the week. The EIA reports a draw of 7 million barrels, a 5 million barrel surprise draw. WTI front month jumps $1.50 within 30 seconds of the release. Gasoline stocks also draw 3 million barrels vs expectations of a 1 million draw. A trader who was long WTI going into the report profits immediately. However, if the EIA had reported a build of 3 million barrels (a 5 million barrel surprise build vs expectations), WTI would likely have dropped $1.50 or more, and the long position would have suffered.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Release schedule:** Wednesday 10:30 AM ET (delayed to Thursday if Monday is a federal holiday).

**Key data points:**
- Crude oil inventories (total US and Cushing separately)
- Gasoline inventories
- Distillate inventories
- Refinery utilization (%)
- Crude oil production (weekly estimate)
- Crude imports and exports
- Days of supply

**Price sensitivity rule of thumb:** A 1 million barrel surprise in crude draws/builds moves WTI approximately $0.20 to $0.40, though sensitivity varies with market context.

## Common misconceptions

**"The EIA report tells you the direction of oil prices."** A single weekly data point does not determine the trend. It creates short term volatility but must be interpreted within the broader supply/demand context.

**"A build is always bearish."** Context matters. A small build during turnaround season (when builds are expected) is actually bullish if the market expected a larger build. The surprise vs consensus is what drives the reaction.

**"EIA data is perfectly accurate."** Weekly data is based on surveys and estimates, subject to significant revisions. Monthly data (published with a lag) is more accurate. The weekly production estimate in particular can be off by hundreds of thousands of barrels per day.
