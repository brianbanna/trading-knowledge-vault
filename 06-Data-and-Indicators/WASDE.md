# WASDE

## Definition
The World Agricultural Supply and Demand Estimates (WASDE) is the USDA's monthly report providing global supply and demand forecasts for [[Corn]], [[Wheat]], [[Soybeans]], [[Rice]], [[Cotton Futures|cotton]], and [[Sugar Futures|sugar]]. Released around the 10th of each month at 12:00 PM ET, it routinely produces extreme volatility in agricultural futures within minutes of publication. WASDE covers beginning stocks, production, imports, domestic use, exports, and ending stocks for each commodity across all major producers and consumers. It is the agricultural equivalent of the EIA Weekly Petroleum Status Report and the benchmark reference for global crop pricing. Compiled by an interagency committee in a lockup to prevent leaks, WASDE numbers set the consensus baseline for global [[Grains]] and oilseeds until the next monthly update.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
WASDE is the single most market moving scheduled event for agricultural futures, routinely causing 3 to 5% swings in [[Corn]], [[Wheat]], and [[Soybeans]] within minutes. The ending stocks figure and implied [[Stocks to Use Ratio]] determine surplus vs deficit and shape the curve from [[Contango Roll Cost|contango]] to [[Backwardation Roll Gain|backwardation]]. For FX traders, outcomes affect major exporter currencies: a bullish corn report strengthens [[BRL/USD]] sentiment via Brazil's export revenue expectations. Commodity trading firms build entire research teams around anticipating WASDE revisions.

## Concrete example
**Concrete:** January 2023, WASDE cuts Argentina soybean production by 8M tonnes on La Nina drought, dropping the global [[Stocks to Use Ratio]] to 23%. Trader long 20 March soybean contracts at $14.80/bu before the report sees soybeans gap to $15.40/bu within 10 minutes. Gain: $0.60/bu × 5,000 bu × 20 = $60,000. Trader had pre positioned based on satellite imagery showing Argentine crop stress USDA had not incorporated.

**Simplified:** Once a month USDA publishes a global balance sheet for major crops. They count how much was grown, used, exported, and what is left over (ending stocks). If they cut production or raise demand more than the market expected, stocks shrink and prices jump. If they revise the other way, prices fall. The market reacts in seconds because grain traders all pre position based on private estimates. Trade the surprise vs consensus, not the headline level.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **Balance sheet identity:** Ending stocks = Beginning stocks + Production + Imports − Domestic use − Exports
- **[[Stocks to Use Ratio]] = Ending stocks / (Domestic use + Exports) × 100**
- **Production = Harvested area × Yield**
- **Surprise = WASDE estimate − Trade consensus** (consensus from pre report Reuters and Bloomberg surveys)
- **Price sensitivity (corn):** 100M bushel surprise in US corn ending stocks moves corn ~$0.10 to $0.15/bu
- **Release schedule:** Monthly, typically 2nd Thursday or Friday at 12:00 PM ET

## Related concepts (learn next)
- [[Stocks to Use Ratio]] is the key derived metric summarizing surplus vs deficit.
- [[Old Crop New Crop]] spread reacts sharply to WASDE changes in current vs next year balance sheets.
- [[Weather Premium]] builds when WASDE yield assumptions are at risk from adverse growing conditions.
- [[COT Positioning]] shows whether speculators have priced in expected WASDE outcomes.
- [[Grains]] are the primary asset class affected by WASDE.
- [[Crush Spread]] reacts when WASDE revises soybean meal or oil estimates.
- [[Implied Volatility]] in grain options spikes into WASDE and collapses after.
- [[Basis Risk]] can rise when national estimates diverge from local supply.

## Common misconceptions

**"WASDE is the final word on supply and demand."** WASDE is revised every month and intra month developments (weather, exports, policy) make any report stale within days. StoneX, AgResource, and Informa frequently disagree.

**"You can trade WASDE by reacting to the headline."** Markets react to the full balance sheet. A bearish production number can be offset by a simultaneous demand cut. Parse beginning stocks, production, demand categories, and exports together.

**"WASDE only matters for US markets."** WASDE includes global estimates for Brazil, Argentina, China, India, EU, and dozens of others. A Brazilian soybean revision can move US futures as much as a domestic revision.
