# China PMI

## Definition

The China Purchasing Managers Index is a monthly survey based indicator of manufacturing activity in China, the world's largest consumer of base metals, second largest economy, and a key driver of global commodity demand. Two main versions are published: the NBS (National Bureau of Statistics) Manufacturing PMI, covering larger state owned enterprises, and the Caixin Manufacturing PMI (compiled by S&P Global), which tilts toward smaller private companies. A reading above 50 indicates expansion, below 50 indicates contraction. The PMI is a leading indicator, typically moving ahead of actual production and GDP data by 1 to 3 months.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Base metals prices are highly sensitive to Chinese PMI data because China consumes approximately 50% of global copper, aluminum, zinc, and nickel. A PMI beat (higher than expected) is immediately bullish for metals, while a miss is bearish. The market reacts to the surprise relative to consensus, not the absolute level. PMI subcomponents (new orders, new export orders, inventories, employment) provide additional granularity. For FX traders, strong China PMI supports commodity currencies ([[Commodity Currencies]] like AUD, NZD) and weakens safe havens. PMI data also feeds into [[SHFE]] pricing and the [[SHFE vs LME Arb]] calculation.

## Concrete example

Consensus expects January 2026 NBS Manufacturing PMI at 49.5 (mild contraction). The actual print comes in at 51.2 (expansion), a significant beat. LME copper rallies $200/tonne within the hour, AUD/USD jumps 40 pips, and iron ore futures on SGX rise 3%. A trader who was long copper and long AUD/USD ahead of the release profits on both legs. If the PMI had printed 48.0 (much weaker than expected), copper could have dropped $300/tonne and AUD/USD fallen 60 pips.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Release schedule:**
- NBS PMI: last day of each month (released at 9:00 AM Beijing time)
- Caixin PMI: 1st business day of the following month (9:45 AM Beijing time)

**Interpretation:**
- Above 50: manufacturing expansion
- Below 50: manufacturing contraction
- Distance from 50 indicates pace of change

**Key subcomponents:**
- New orders: forward looking demand indicator
- New export orders: external demand
- Inventories: restocking vs destocking
- Prices paid: input cost inflation

**Metal sensitivity:** A 1 point PMI beat typically moves LME copper $100 to $200/tonne and iron ore 2 to 4%.

## Common misconceptions

**"Only the headline PMI number matters."** Subcomponents (new orders, inventories) often provide a more nuanced signal than the headline. The headline can be misleading if driven by non demand factors.

**"NBS and Caixin PMI always agree."** They can diverge because they survey different company sizes. When they diverge, it reveals a bifurcation between large SOEs and smaller private companies.

**"Strong PMI means strong metal demand immediately."** PMI is a leading indicator of activity, not actual consumption. There can be a lag between PMI improvement and physical metal demand showing up in the market.
