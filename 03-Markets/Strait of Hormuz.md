# Strait of Hormuz

## Definition

The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow waterway between Iran and Oman connecting the Persian Gulf to the Gulf of Oman and the open ocean. It is approximately 33 km wide at its narrowest point, with shipping lanes only about 3 km wide in each direction. Roughly 20% of global oil supply (approximately 21 million barrels per day) and about 25% of global LNG trade passes through it. It is the single most important chokepoint in global energy markets.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Any disruption to Strait of Hormuz transit, whether actual or threatened, causes immediate spikes in [[Brent Crude]], [[WTI]], and [[LNG]] prices. The strait is the supply chain bottleneck for Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait, UAE, and Qatar. Energy traders must monitor geopolitical tension in the region constantly. Even a credible threat of closure (Iranian military exercises, tanker seizures) adds a risk premium of $5 to $15 per barrel to crude prices.

## Concrete example

**Scenario: Iran threatens to close the strait following new sanctions.**

A trader is watching [[Brent Crude]] at $78/barrel. Iran's Revolutionary Guard announces naval exercises near the strait and threatens "consequences" if sanctions tighten.

**Success case:** The trader immediately buys 10 Brent contracts (10,000 barrels) at $78. Over the next 3 days, the risk premium pushes Brent to $86. The trader sells at $85, capturing $7/barrel * 10,000 = $70,000 profit.

**Failure case:** The trader buys at $78 on the headlines. The US and Iran quietly resume negotiations the next day. The risk premium evaporates, Brent drops to $75, and the trader loses $3/barrel * 10,000 = $30,000.

**Historical precedent:** In January 2020, following the US killing of Iranian General Soleimani, Brent spiked from $66 to $71 overnight (7.6%), then reversed most of the move within a week as tensions de escalated.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Key statistics:**
- Width at narrowest: ~33 km (21 miles)
- Shipping lane width: ~3 km each direction
- Daily oil flow: ~21 million barrels/day (~20% of global supply)
- Daily LNG flow: ~25% of global LNG trade
- Key exporters transiting: Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait, UAE, Qatar

**Risk premium estimation:**

Crude risk premium = probability_of_disruption * expected_price_impact * expected_duration

Example: 10% chance of 2 week disruption causing $30/barrel spike = $30 * 0.10 = $3/barrel embedded risk premium

**Alternative routes (and their limitations):**
- East West Pipeline (Saudi): 5M bbl/day capacity, bypasses Hormuz
- Abu Dhabi ADCOP pipeline: 1.5M bbl/day, bypasses Hormuz
- Total alternative capacity: ~6.5M bbl/day vs. 21M bbl/day transiting, leaving ~14.5M bbl/day at risk

## Common misconceptions

1. **"Closing the strait would only affect oil."** Qatar is the world's largest LNG exporter, and all its cargoes transit Hormuz. A closure would simultaneously spike [[LNG]] prices globally, with cascading effects on European and Asian power markets.

2. **"Iran would never actually close it."** Iran's own oil exports transit the strait, so full closure is self destructive. But partial disruption (harassing tankers, mining, seizing vessels) is entirely plausible and has happened multiple times (Tanker War 1984 to 1988, 2019 tanker seizures).

3. **"The price spike from a disruption is permanent."** Historically, Hormuz related spikes reverse quickly once the threat subsides. Trading these events requires fast entry and disciplined exit, not a buy and hold approach.
