# Strait of Hormuz

## Definition

The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow waterway between Iran and Oman connecting the Persian Gulf to the Gulf of Oman and the open ocean. ~33 km wide at the narrowest point, with shipping lanes only ~3 km wide each direction. Roughly 20% of global oil supply (~21 million barrels/day) and ~25% of global LNG trade transit it. It is the single most important chokepoint in global energy markets.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Any disruption, actual or threatened, causes immediate spikes in [[Brent Crude]], [[WTI]], and [[LNG]] prices. The strait is the supply chain bottleneck for Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait, UAE, and Qatar. Energy traders monitor regional geopolitical tension constantly. Even a credible closure threat (Iranian military exercises, tanker seizures) adds $5 to $15/barrel of risk premium to crude.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** Iran threatens to close the strait following new sanctions. [[Brent Crude]] at $78/barrel. Iran's Revolutionary Guard announces naval exercises near the strait and threatens consequences if sanctions tighten. Trader buys 10 Brent contracts at $78. Over 3 days the risk premium pushes Brent to $86. Trader sells at $85. Gain: $7 × 1,000 bbl × 10 = $70,000. Counter scenario: US and Iran quietly resume negotiations the next day. Risk premium evaporates. Brent drops to $75. Loss: $3 × 1,000 × 10 = $30,000. Historical: January 2020, after the Soleimani killing, Brent spiked from $66 to $71 overnight (7.6%), then reversed most of the move within a week as tensions de escalated.

**Simplified:** A fifth of the world's oil travels through a strip of water 33 km wide between Iran and Oman. Every time Iran rattles a saber or seizes a tanker, traders price in a small probability that the strait closes. Crude jumps $5 to $15 within hours. If the threat fades, the premium evaporates. Trading Hormuz is binary and fast: get in quickly on credible threats, exit quickly when tensions cool, do not hold through resolution.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Key statistics:**
- Width at narrowest: ~33 km (21 miles)
- Shipping lane width: ~3 km each direction
- Daily oil flow: ~21 million bbl/day (~20% of global supply)
- Daily LNG flow: ~25% of global LNG trade
- Key exporters transiting: Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait, UAE, Qatar

**Risk premium estimation:**

Crude risk premium = probability_of_disruption × expected_price_impact × expected_duration

Example: 10% chance of a 2 week disruption causing a $30/bbl spike = $30 × 0.10 = $3/bbl embedded risk premium

**Alternative routes (and limits):**
- East West Pipeline (Saudi): 5M bbl/day capacity, bypasses Hormuz
- Abu Dhabi ADCOP pipeline: 1.5M bbl/day, bypasses Hormuz
- Total alternative capacity: ~6.5M bbl/day vs 21M bbl/day transiting, leaving ~14.5M bbl/day at risk

## Common misconceptions

**"Closing the strait would only affect oil."** Qatar is the world's largest LNG exporter and all its cargoes transit Hormuz. A closure spikes [[LNG]] globally with cascading effects on European and Asian power markets.

**"Iran would never actually close it."** Iran's own oil exports transit the strait, so full closure is self destructive. Partial disruption (harassing tankers, mining, seizing vessels) is entirely plausible and has happened (Tanker War 1984 to 1988, 2019 tanker seizures).

**"The price spike from a disruption is permanent."** Historically, Hormuz spikes reverse quickly once the threat subsides. Trade these with fast entry and disciplined exit, not buy and hold.
