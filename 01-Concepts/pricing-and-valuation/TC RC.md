# TC RC

## Definition

TC/RC stands for Treatment Charge and Refining Charge, the fees miners pay smelters for converting [[Concentrate]] (raw ore) into refined metal ([[Cathode|copper cathode]]). TC is charged per dry metric tonne of concentrate; RC is charged per pound of contained metal. Together they are smelter revenue for processing services. TC/RCs are negotiated annually between miners and smelters (the benchmark) and trade on a spot basis. They are the key indicator of the balance between concentrate supply and smelting capacity.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

TC/RCs reveal which side of the concentrate market holds bargaining power. High TC/RCs mean smelters are in the driver's seat because concentrate is abundant and miners compete for limited capacity. Low TC/RCs mean miners have power because concentrate is scarce. This directly affects refined supply: if TC/RCs collapse and smelters become unprofitable, they curtail operations, reducing refined output and tightening the refined market. For copper, the TC/RC cycle is 1 of the most important medium term fundamental indicators.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** 2026 annual copper TC/RC benchmark set at $80/tonne and $0.08/lb (TC $80, RC 8 cents). Large Chilean mine suffers a disruption, concentrate supply drops. Spot TC/RCs collapse to $30/5 cents as smelters compete for scarce feed. Chinese smelters earning $30/5 are below breakeven ($50/5) and begin curtailing. Reduced smelter output tightens refined copper, LME rises $400/tonne over 3 months. Trader who tracked the TC/RC collapse as a leading indicator went long copper early at $9,400, exited at $9,800, earning $400/tonne on 50 lots = $500,000.

**Simplified:** Mines dig up rock with copper in it (concentrate). Smelters turn that rock into pure copper. They charge a fee for the service. When mines produce a lot of rock and smelters have limited capacity, smelters charge high fees. When rock is scarce, smelters fight for it and fees collapse. Collapsing fees mean smelters might shut down, which means less pure copper coming out, which is bullish for copper prices later.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**TC/RC convention:** TC = $/dry metric tonne of concentrate. RC = cents/lb of payable metal.

**Example:** TC $80/DMT, RC 8.0 cents/lb for copper concentrate with 25% copper content.

**Smelter revenue per tonne of concentrate:**
`Revenue ≈ (Contained Metal × LME Price × Payable %) - (Contained Metal × RC) + TC`

**Breakeven TC/RC:** Varies by smelter, $40 to $60 TC / 4 to 6 cents/lb RC for Chinese copper smelters.

**Annual benchmark:** Set in Q4 each year for the following calendar year, negotiated between major miners (Freeport, BHP, Codelco) and smelters (Jiangxi Copper, Aurubis).

## Common misconceptions

**"Low TC/RCs are bearish for copper."** Low TC/RCs mean concentrate is scarce, which means less refined output, which is bullish for refined copper.

**"TC/RCs only matter for mining and smelting companies."** Critical fundamental indicator for any copper trader because they forecast refined supply changes 3 to 6 months ahead.

**"The annual benchmark is what everyone pays."** Many contracts price off spot TC/RCs, which can diverge significantly from the benchmark.
