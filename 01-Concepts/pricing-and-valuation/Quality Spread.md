# Quality Spread

## Definition

A quality spread is the price difference between 2 crude grades of different quality, measured along the [[API Gravity]] axis (light vs heavy) or the [[Sweet vs Sour]] axis (sulfur content). Common spreads: light/heavy (Brent vs Maya), sweet/sour (Brent vs Dubai). These differentials trade as their own markets and reflect refinery economics, environmental regulation, and complex processing capacity. Widening quality spreads mean the market pays more for easier to refine grades. Narrowing spreads signal abundant complex capacity or tightness in the lower quality grade.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Quality spreads are essential for physical traders, refiners, and relative value desks. A refiner picking between 2 grades compares the quality spread against processing cost differences. When regulations tighten (IMO 2020), sweet premiums widen because low sulfur products become more valuable. When a heavy sour producer is disrupted (Venezuela, Iran), the heavy sour discount narrows. Financial traders use quality spreads to express refinery views with lower vol than flat price.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** Brent/Maya quality spread at $16/bbl (Brent $82, Maya $66) versus a 5 year average of $10. Trader believes the spread is wide because Gulf Coast coking capacity is offline for turnarounds, reducing heavy sour demand. She shorts Brent and goes long Maya equivalent. Cokers restart in May, spread narrows to $11, earning $5/bbl. If Mexican Maya supply drops further, spread narrows faster. If a second coker goes down, spread widens to $20, losing $4/bbl.

**Simplified:** Refineries pay more for crude that is easy to turn into gasoline and diesel. Light, low sulfur crude is easy. Heavy, high sulfur crude requires expensive processing. The price gap between the 2 widens when easy crude is scarce or when only simple refineries are running. It narrows when complex refineries are humming and want the cheap heavy stuff.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Quality spread:** `Quality Spread = Light Sweet Price - Heavy Sour Price`

**Common quality spreads:**
- Brent/Dubai (sweet/sour): global sweet vs sour balance
- LLS/Mars (US Gulf Coast light/heavy): local quality differential
- Brent/Urals: light sweet vs Russian medium sour
- WTI/WCS: light sweet vs Canadian heavy

**Drivers of widening:**
- Environmental regulation tightening
- Complex refinery outages (cokers, hydrocrackers)
- Heavy sour supply abundance
- Light sweet supply tightness

## Common misconceptions

**"Quality spreads only reflect crude quality."** Logistics, refinery configuration, and geopolitics all influence them. The same quality differential trades at different levels in different regions.

**"Wide quality spreads always mean revert."** Structural changes (new regulations, permanent refinery closures, grade specific production declines) shift the long run average.

**"Quality spreads are stable."** Brent/Dubai has ranged $1 to $10 in recent years. Light/heavy spreads have ranged wider during stress.
