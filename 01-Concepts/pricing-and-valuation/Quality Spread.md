# Quality Spread

## Definition

A quality spread is the price difference between two crude oil grades of different quality, typically measured along the [[API Gravity]] axis (light vs heavy) or the [[Sweet vs Sour]] axis (sulfur content). The most common quality spreads are light/heavy (e.g., Brent vs Maya) and sweet/sour (e.g., Brent vs Dubai). These differentials are tradeable markets in their own right and reflect refinery economics, environmental regulations, and the availability of complex processing capacity. Widening quality spreads mean the market is placing a higher premium on easier to refine grades, while narrowing spreads signal abundant complex refinery capacity or tightness in the lower quality grade.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Quality spreads are essential for physical traders, refiners, and relative value desks. A refiner choosing between two crude grades must compare the quality spread against processing cost differences. When environmental regulations tighten (like IMO 2020), sweet premiums widen because low sulfur products become more valuable. When a major heavy sour producer faces disruptions (Venezuela, Iran), the heavy sour discount narrows as supply tightens. For financial traders, quality spreads offer a lower volatility way to express views on refinery economics without taking flat price risk.

## Concrete example

A trader observes the Brent/Maya quality spread at $16/bbl (Brent at $82, Maya at $66). The 5 year average is $10. She believes the spread is wide because Gulf Coast coking capacity is offline for turnarounds, reducing heavy sour demand. As refineries return from maintenance in May, she expects the spread to narrow. She goes short Brent and long Maya equivalent exposure. If the spread narrows to $11 as cokers restart, she profits $5/bbl. If instead Mexican Maya production declines (tightening heavy sour supply further), the spread could narrow even faster. But if a second coker goes offline, the spread could widen to $20, losing $4/bbl.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Quality spread:** `Quality Spread = Light Sweet Price - Heavy Sour Price`

**Common quality spreads:**
- Brent/Dubai (sweet/sour): reflects global sweet vs sour balance
- LLS/Mars (US Gulf Coast light/heavy): local quality differential
- Brent/Urals: light sweet vs Russian medium sour
- WTI/WCS: light sweet vs Canadian heavy

**Drivers of widening:**
- Environmental regulation tightening
- Complex refinery outages (cokers, hydrocrackers)
- Heavy sour supply abundance
- Light sweet supply tightness

## Common misconceptions

**"Quality spreads only reflect crude quality."** Logistics, refinery configuration, and geopolitics all influence quality spreads. The same quality differential can trade at very different levels in different regions.

**"Wide quality spreads are always mean reverting."** Structural changes (new environmental regulations, permanent refinery closures, production declines in specific grades) can shift the long run average.

**"Quality spreads are stable."** The Brent/Dubai spread has ranged from $1 to $10 in recent years. Light/heavy spreads have ranged even wider during periods of market stress.
