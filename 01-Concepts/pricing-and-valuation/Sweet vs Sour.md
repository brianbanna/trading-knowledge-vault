# Sweet vs Sour

## Definition

Sweet and sour are crude oil classifications by sulfur content. Sweet crude has less than 0.5% sulfur by weight and is easier and cheaper to refine into gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel. Sour crude has more than 0.5% sulfur and requires energy intensive desulfurization (hydrotreating) to meet product specs and environmental standards. The terms originated in early oil trading when prospectors tasted crude: low sulfur tasted mildly sweet, high sulfur was bitter. Along with [[API Gravity]], sulfur content is the most important quality characteristic determining crude value.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

The sweet/sour differential is 1 of the most actively traded quality spreads in oil. When regulations tighten (IMO 2020 requiring ships to burn fuel < 0.5% sulfur), demand for sweet crude and low sulfur products surges, widening the sweet premium. When complex refinery capacity (desulfurization units) is abundant, refiners process sour profitably, narrowing the differential. Traders express views on refinery economics, environmental policy, and regional disruptions through sweet/sour spreads. Sovereign impact: sweet producers (Nigeria, Libya, Algeria) gain from tighter standards; sour producers (Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Venezuela) face relative discounts.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** Trader tracks sweet/sour spread using [[Brent Crude]] (0.4% sulfur) vs [[Dubai Crude]] (2.0% sulfur). Q3 2025 spread at $4/bbl, near the 5 year average. Trader expects IMO compliance enforcement to tighten in 2026, increasing demand for low sulfur marine fuel and sweet crude. Goes long Brent, short Dubai (buys the [[EFS]] spread). Spread widens to $7/bbl, earning $3/bbl. If OPEC+ cuts disproportionately reduce sour supply, Dubai tightens, spread compresses to $2/bbl, losing $2/bbl.

**Simplified:** Crude oil contains sulfur. Refineries hate sulfur because it has to be removed to make legal fuel. Low sulfur crude (sweet) is more valuable because the refining is easy. High sulfur crude (sour) sells at a discount because only refineries with the right equipment can profitably process it. The size of that discount swings with environmental rules and refinery capacity.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Sulfur classification:**
- Sweet: < 0.5% sulfur
- Medium sour: 0.5% to 1.5% sulfur
- Sour: > 1.5% sulfur

**Sweet/sour differential:** `Sweet Premium = Sweet Crude Price - Sour Crude Price`

**Desulfurization cost:** Hydrotreating sour crude adds $2 to $5/bbl in refining costs depending on sulfur level and refinery configuration.

**Key benchmark sulfur levels:**
- WTI: ~0.24% (sweet)
- Brent: ~0.37% (sweet)
- Dubai: ~2.0% (sour)
- Mars (US Gulf): ~1.9% (sour)
- Urals: ~1.3% (medium sour)

## Common misconceptions

**"Sour crude is bad crude."** Sour crude is highly profitable for complex refineries with desulfurization capacity. The sour discount more than compensates for processing cost.

**"Sweet/sour differentials are stable."** They swing dramatically with environmental regulation, refinery outages, and OPEC production mix shifts. IMO 2020 caused some of the largest sweet/sour moves in decades.

**"All sweet crudes are interchangeable."** Sweet crudes vary in API gravity, naphtha content, and other characteristics. Nigerian Bonny Light and Algerian Saharan Blend are both sweet with different refinery yields.
