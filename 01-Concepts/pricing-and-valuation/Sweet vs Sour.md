# Sweet vs Sour

## Definition

Sweet and sour are classifications of crude oil based on sulfur content. Sweet crude contains less than 0.5% sulfur by weight and is easier and cheaper to refine into clean burning products like gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel. Sour crude contains more than 0.5% sulfur and requires energy intensive desulfurization (hydrotreating) to meet product specifications and environmental standards. The terms originated in early oil trading when prospectors would taste crude: low sulfur oil tasted mildly sweet, while high sulfur oil had a bitter, sulfurous taste. Along with [[API Gravity]], sulfur content is the most important quality characteristic determining crude oil value.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

The sweet/sour differential is one of the most actively traded quality spreads in oil markets. When environmental regulations tighten (such as IMO 2020 requiring ships to burn fuel with < 0.5% sulfur), demand for sweet crude and low sulfur products surges, widening the sweet premium. When complex refinery capacity (desulfurization units) is abundant, refiners can profitably process sour crude, narrowing the differential. Traders express views on refinery economics, environmental policy, and regional supply disruptions through sweet/sour spreads. The differential also affects sovereign economics: countries producing sweet crude (Nigeria, Libya, Algeria) benefit from tighter environmental standards, while sour crude producers (Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Venezuela) face relative discounts.

## Concrete example

A trader tracks the sweet/sour spread using [[Brent Crude]] (0.4% sulfur, sweet) vs [[Dubai Crude]] (2.0% sulfur, sour). In Q3 2025, the spread is $4/bbl, near the 5 year average. The trader expects IMO compliance enforcement to tighten in 2026, increasing demand for low sulfur marine fuel and the sweet crude needed to produce it. She goes long Brent and short Dubai (buying the [[EFS]] spread). If the sweet/sour spread widens to $7/bbl, she profits $3/bbl. If instead OPEC+ cuts disproportionately reduce sour supply, Dubai tightens and the spread compresses to $2/bbl, she loses $2/bbl.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Sulfur classification:**
- Sweet: < 0.5% sulfur
- Medium sour: 0.5% to 1.5% sulfur
- Sour: > 1.5% sulfur

**Sweet/sour differential:** `Sweet Premium = Sweet Crude Price - Sour Crude Price`

**Desulfurization cost:** Hydrotreating sour crude adds approximately $2 to $5/bbl in refining costs depending on sulfur level and refinery configuration.

**Key benchmark sulfur levels:**
- WTI: ~0.24% (sweet)
- Brent: ~0.37% (sweet)
- Dubai: ~2.0% (sour)
- Mars (US Gulf): ~1.9% (sour)
- Urals: ~1.3% (medium sour)

## Common misconceptions

**"Sour crude is bad crude."** Sour crude can be highly profitable for complex refineries that have invested in desulfurization. The sour discount often more than compensates for the processing cost.

**"Sweet/sour differentials are stable."** They can swing dramatically with environmental regulation changes, refinery outages, and OPEC production mix shifts. The IMO 2020 transition caused some of the largest sweet/sour moves in decades.

**"All sweet crudes are interchangeable."** Sweet crudes vary significantly in API gravity, naphtha content, and other characteristics. Nigerian Bonny Light and Algerian Saharan Blend are both sweet but have different refinery yields.
