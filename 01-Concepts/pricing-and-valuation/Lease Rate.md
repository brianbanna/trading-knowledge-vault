# Lease Rate

## Definition

The gold lease rate is the interest rate charged for borrowing physical gold from a lender (typically a central bank or bullion bank) for a specified period. Central banks earn a return on their gold reserves by lending gold to commercial banks, who use it for short selling, hedging, or satisfying physical delivery obligations. The lease rate is derived from the relationship between the gold forward rate ([[GOFO]]) and the dollar interest rate (LIBOR/SOFR): Lease Rate = Dollar Rate minus GOFO. When the lease rate spikes, it signals that physical gold is scarce and borrowers are competing for metal. Low lease rates indicate ample gold supply in the lending market.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Spiking gold lease rates are a signal of physical gold scarcity and potential short squeeze conditions. When lease rates rise sharply, it means borrowers cannot easily find physical gold, which can indicate strong physical demand, delivery stress on exchanges, or a reduction in central bank lending. Historically, lease rate spikes have preceded or coincided with gold price rallies as physical tightness translates to higher prices. For traders, monitoring lease rates provides an early warning system for physical market stress that futures prices alone may not capture.

## Concrete example

Gold 1 month lease rates spike from 0.1% to 2.5% over a week. A trader interprets this as evidence that physical gold is scarce (possibly due to heavy ETF inflows or central bank demand). She goes long gold at $2,400. The physical tightness drives gold to $2,500 as borrowers who need metal are forced to buy outright rather than lease. Profit: $100/oz. If the lease rate spike had been caused by a temporary logistics issue (gold stuck in transit) rather than genuine scarcity, rates normalize quickly and the trade opportunity evaporates.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Lease rate derivation:** `Lease Rate = Dollar Interest Rate (SOFR) - GOFO`

**Typical gold lease rates:** 0.05% to 0.50% (normal), 1 to 3% (elevated), > 3% (extreme tightness).

**Who lends gold:** Central banks (largest lenders), bullion banks, sovereign wealth funds.

**Who borrows gold:** Banks needing physical for delivery, miners hedging forward production (historical), short sellers.

## Common misconceptions

**"Lease rates are publicly quoted like LIBOR."** Gold lease rates are not formally published as a benchmark. They are derived from GOFO and dollar rates, or inferred from OTC market transactions.

**"High lease rates always mean gold will rally."** Lease rate spikes can be caused by temporary technical factors (settlement bottlenecks) rather than genuine scarcity.

**"Central banks always lend their gold."** Some central banks have reduced lending or repatriated gold reserves, reducing the pool of lendable gold and structurally supporting lease rates.
