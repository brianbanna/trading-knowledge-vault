# Cable

## Definition
Cable is the market slang term for the GBP/USD [[currency pair]]. The name dates back to the mid-1800s when the exchange rate between the British pound and the US dollar was transmitted across the Atlantic via the transatlantic telegraph cable laid in 1866. The term stuck and remains universal on every trading desk, interbank chat, and voice broker line. When a trader says "cable is bid at 50" they mean GBP/USD is being bought at a price ending in 50 (e.g., 1.2650). Understanding this jargon is essential because market commentary, broker runs, and interbank communication use "cable" exclusively rather than "GBP/USD."

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
GBP/USD is one of the most liquid [[G10 Currencies]] pairs and a core trading instrument on every FX desk. For commodity traders, cable matters because London is a global hub for metals ([[LME]]), energy (ICE Brent), and soft commodities. Revenue generated in GBP from UK-based commodity operations must be converted, and hedging GBP exposure requires fluency in cable dynamics. Cable is also a key cross-rate for constructing positions in [[EUR/GBP]] and other sterling crosses. The pair is heavily influenced by [[Bank of England]] policy, UK economic data (GDP, CPI, employment), and Brexit-related political risk. Daily average volume in GBP/USD exceeds $400 billion.

## Concrete example
A metals trading firm sells 10,000 tonnes of copper through the [[LME]], receiving payment in USD. The firm's costs are in GBP (London office, UK operations). The CFO instructs the FX desk to buy 80 million GBP/USD (buy GBP, sell USD) to cover the next quarter's GBP expenses. Cable is trading at 1.2650/1.2652. The desk buys 80 million at 1.2652, paying $101,216,000 to receive GBP 80,000,000.

The win: the Bank of England unexpectedly raises rates the following week, cable rallies to 1.2800, and the firm locked in a better rate. The fail: UK GDP data comes in weak, cable drops to 1.2450, and the firm could have bought GBP 2 pips cheaper per unit, costing an extra $1,600,000 (80,000,000 x 0.0200) on the hedge. This illustrates why timing and hedging strategy for cable exposure matters.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **Quotation convention**: Cable is quoted as GBP per 1 USD is incorrect. Cable is quoted as USD per 1 GBP. A quote of 1.2650 means 1 GBP = 1.2650 USD.
- **Pip value**: 1 pip in cable = 0.0001. On a 1 million GBP position, 1 pip = $100.
- **Primary trading venues**: [[Reuters Matching]] is the dominant [[ECN]] for cable. [[EBS]] has secondary liquidity.
- **Typical spread**: 0.1 to 0.5 pips in interbank during London hours, widening to 1 to 3 pips during Asian session.
- **Key trading sessions**: Most liquid during London hours (07:00 to 16:00 GMT), with a secondary spike during US overlap (13:00 to 16:00 GMT).
- **Cross-rate relationship**: EUR/GBP = EUR/USD / GBP/USD. Cable and [[Fiber]] together determine the EUR/GBP cross.

## Related concepts (learn next)

- [[EUR/GBP]]: the cross rate derived from cable and [[Fiber]], heavily traded for relative UK vs. Eurozone positioning.
- [[Bank of England]]: the central bank whose interest rate decisions are the single largest driver of cable.
- [[Reuters Matching]]: the primary [[ECN]] for cable interbank trading.
- [[FX Swap]]: cable FX swaps are among the most liquid instruments for managing GBP funding and hedging.
- [[Commodity Currencies]]: cable is not classified as a commodity currency, but UK commodity firms still have significant GBP/USD exposure.
- [[Big Figure]]: in cable, the big figure might be "1.26," and traders quote only the last 2 digits ("50/52" meaning 1.2650/1.2652).

## Common misconceptions
1. **"Cable is just another major pair"**: Cable has unique characteristics including sensitivity to UK political risk (Brexit, Scottish independence), linkage to London commodity markets, and its own liquidity profile that differs from EUR/USD. It often moves on UK-specific headlines that do not affect other G10 pairs.
2. **"Cable and sterling are different things"**: They are the same. Cable = GBP/USD = sterling-dollar. "Sterling" refers to GBP generally, while "cable" specifically refers to the GBP/USD exchange rate. On a desk, the terms are used interchangeably.
3. **"Cable liquidity is the same 24 hours"**: Cable is heavily concentrated in the London session. Spreads widen 3 to 5x during the Asian session, and significant cable moves after London hours are unusual unless driven by a major news event.
