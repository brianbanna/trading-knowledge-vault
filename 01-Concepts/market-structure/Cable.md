# Cable

## Definition
Cable is the market slang for the GBP/USD [[currency pair]]. The name dates to the mid 1800s, when the GBP/USD rate was transmitted across the Atlantic via the transatlantic telegraph cable laid in 1866. The term stuck and remains universal on every desk, interbank chat, and voice broker line. "Cable is bid at 50" means GBP/USD has buyers at a price ending in 50 (e.g., 1.2650). Understanding this jargon matters because market commentary, broker runs, and interbank chat use "cable" exclusively, not "GBP/USD."

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
GBP/USD is one of the most liquid [[G10 Currencies]] pairs and a core instrument on every FX desk. For commodity traders, cable matters because London is a global hub for metals ([[LME]]), energy (ICE Brent), and softs. GBP revenues from UK based commodity operations must be converted, and hedging GBP exposure requires fluency in cable. Cable is also a key cross rate for constructing [[EUR/GBP]] and other sterling crosses. The pair is heavily driven by [[Bank of England]] policy, UK data (GDP, CPI, employment), and Brexit related political risk. Daily volume exceeds $400 billion.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** A metals firm sells 10,000 tonnes of copper through the [[LME]], receiving USD. Costs are in GBP. CFO instructs the FX desk to buy GBP 80 million against USD to cover next quarter's GBP expenses. Cable at 1.2650/1.2652. Desk buys 80 million at 1.2652, paying $101,216,000. BOE then unexpectedly hikes; cable rallies to 1.2800. Firm locked in the better rate. Mirror case: weak UK GDP drops cable to 1.2450 the week after the hedge, costing $1,600,000 (80,000,000 × 0.0200) in opportunity cost vs waiting.

**Simplified:** Cable is the nickname for the British pound versus the US dollar. It is a major FX pair with deep liquidity and tight spreads during London hours. London commodity firms with GBP costs and USD revenues must trade cable constantly. Bank of England decisions and UK data are the biggest drivers. The name comes from a 19th century telegraph cable across the Atlantic, and it stuck.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **Quotation**: Cable is quoted as USD per 1 GBP. 1.2650 means 1 GBP = 1.2650 USD.
- **Pip value**: 1 pip = 0.0001. On GBP 1 million, 1 pip = $100.
- **Primary venues**: [[Reuters Matching]] is the dominant [[ECN]]. [[EBS]] has secondary liquidity.
- **Typical spread**: 0.1 to 0.5 pips interbank in London hours, 1 to 3 pips in Asian session.
- **Key sessions**: Most liquid London (07:00 to 16:00 GMT), peak in US overlap (13:00 to 16:00 GMT).
- **Cross relationship**: EUR/GBP = EUR/USD / GBP/USD. Cable and [[Fiber]] together set EUR/GBP.

## Common misconceptions

**"Cable is just another major pair."** Cable has unique sensitivity to UK political risk (Brexit, Scottish independence), London commodity linkages, and a distinct liquidity profile. It moves on UK only headlines that miss other G10 pairs.

**"Cable and sterling are different."** Same. Cable = GBP/USD = sterling dollar. "Sterling" refers to GBP broadly; "cable" specifically to the GBP/USD rate. Used interchangeably on desks.

**"Cable liquidity is the same 24 hours."** Liquidity concentrates in London. Spreads widen 3 to 5x in Asia; significant cable moves after London hours are unusual unless news driven.
