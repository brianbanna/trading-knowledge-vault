# Concentrate

## Definition

Concentrate is the intermediate product from crushing, grinding, and processing (flotation or similar) mined ore to raise metal content. Copper concentrate contains 25 to 35% copper, zinc 45 to 55%, lead 50 to 70%. The remainder is gangue (waste rock), sulfur, iron, and trace elements. Concentrate must be smelted and refined to produce tradeable metal ([[Cathode]] for copper, ingots for zinc and lead). The concentrate market is distinct from the refined metal market with its own supply/demand dynamics, pricing ([[TC RC]]), and logistics.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

The concentrate market is where mine supply meets processing capacity. Tightness or surplus leads refined metal availability by 3 to 6 months (the smelting and refining lag). When concentrate is scarce (mine disruptions, grade decline), [[TC RC]]s fall and smelters compete for feed. Refined output eventually drops and the refined metal market tightens. Monitoring concentrate conditions (TC/RC trends, mine disruption reports, smelter maintenance) gives an early warning system for refined metal price moves.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** A major copper mine in Peru suffers a landslide, taking 200,000 tonnes per year off the market. Spot TC/RCs drop from $70/7 to $35/3.5 as smelters scramble for feed. Within 3 months Chinese smelters cut utilization from 90% to 80%, reducing refined output. LME copper rises $500/tonne as the refined market tightens. A trader who tracked the concentrate disruption and TC/RC collapse went long copper ahead of the move.

**Simplified:** Concentrate is the dirty intermediate product that comes out of a mine. It still needs smelting and refining to become tradeable metal. The market for concentrate is separate from the market for refined metal. When mines have less concentrate, smelters fight for what is available and pay more for it, then eventually produce less refined metal, which raises refined prices a few months later. Watching concentrate tightness predicts refined tightness.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Concentrate metal content:** Expressed as percentage of contained metal.
- Copper concentrate: 25 to 35% Cu
- Zinc concentrate: 45 to 55% Zn
- Lead concentrate: 50 to 70% Pb

**Pricing:** `Concentrate Value = Payable Metal × LME Price − TC/RC − Penalty Elements`

Penalty elements (arsenic, mercury, bismuth) above threshold levels deduct value.

**Payable percentage:** Smelters pay for a percentage of contained metal (96 to 97% for copper).

## Common misconceptions

**"Concentrate and refined are the same market."** Distinct supply/demand dynamics. Concentrate can be tight while refined is in surplus (if smelters drew down concentrate stocks).

**"More mine production means more refined metal."** Only if smelting capacity exists. Concentrate bottlenecks delay translation of mine supply into refined metal.

**"Concentrate quality does not matter."** Penalty elements, moisture, and grade affect smelter economics and effective TC/RC.
