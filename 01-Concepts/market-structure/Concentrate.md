# Concentrate

## Definition

Concentrate is the intermediate product created when mined ore is crushed, ground, and processed (through flotation or other methods) to increase the metal content. Copper concentrate typically contains 25 to 35% copper, zinc concentrate 45 to 55% zinc, and lead concentrate 50 to 70% lead. The remaining material is gangue (waste rock), sulfur, iron, and trace elements. Concentrate must be smelted and refined to produce tradeable metal ([[Cathode]] for copper, ingots for zinc/lead). The concentrate market is a separate market from the refined metal market, with its own supply/demand dynamics, pricing conventions ([[TC RC]]), and logistics.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

The concentrate market is where mine supply meets processing capacity. Tightness or surplus in concentrate supply is a leading indicator for refined metal availability 3 to 6 months later (the typical lag for smelting and refining). When concentrate is scarce (mine disruptions, grade decline), [[TC RC]]s fall and smelters compete for feed. This eventually reduces refined output and tightens the refined metal market. For traders, monitoring concentrate market conditions (TC/RC trends, mine disruption reports, smelter maintenance schedules) provides an early warning system for refined metal price moves.

## Concrete example

A major copper mine in Peru suffers a landslide, taking 200,000 tonnes per year of copper in concentrate off the market. Spot TC/RCs drop from $70/7 to $35/3.5 as smelters scramble for feed. Within 3 months, Chinese smelters begin cutting utilization rates from 90% to 80%, reducing refined copper output. LME copper prices rise $500/tonne as the refined market tightens. A trader who tracked the concentrate disruption and the subsequent TC/RC collapse went long copper ahead of the price move.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Concentrate metal content:** Typically expressed as percentage of contained metal.
- Copper concentrate: 25 to 35% Cu
- Zinc concentrate: 45 to 55% Zn
- Lead concentrate: 50 to 70% Pb

**Pricing:** `Concentrate Value = Payable Metal × LME Price - TC/RC - Penalty Elements`

Penalty elements (arsenic, mercury, bismuth) above threshold levels incur deductions.

**Payable percentage:** Smelters pay for a percentage of the contained metal (typically 96 to 97% for copper).

## Common misconceptions

**"Concentrate and refined metal are the same market."** They have distinct supply/demand dynamics. Concentrate can be tight while refined metal is in surplus (if smelters drew down concentrate stocks) or vice versa.

**"More mine production always means more refined metal."** Only if smelting capacity is available. Concentrate bottlenecks can delay the translation of mine supply into refined metal.

**"Concentrate quality doesn't matter."** Penalty elements, moisture content, and metal grade all affect smelter economics and the effective TC/RC.
