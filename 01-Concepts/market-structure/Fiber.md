# Fiber

## Definition
Fiber (also spelled fibre) is the market slang term for the EUR/USD [[currency pair]]. The name originated in the early 2000s when the euro was introduced, referencing the fiber optic cables that by then had replaced the old telegraph cables used for the original "[[Cable]]" (GBP/USD). EUR/USD is the single most traded currency pair in the world, accounting for roughly 23% of all FX turnover. When a trader says "fiber is offered at 80" they mean EUR/USD has sellers at a price ending in 80 (e.g., 1.0880). The term is common on interbank desks, though less universally adopted than "cable" for GBP/USD.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
EUR/USD is the benchmark FX pair and the most liquid instrument in the world by daily volume (over $700 billion per day). For commodity traders, EUR/USD exposure arises constantly: European refiners buying crude oil priced in USD, commodity trading houses based in Switzerland or the Netherlands converting USD revenues to EUR, and cross-border metals transactions. EUR/USD is also the primary driver of the [[DXY Correlation]] (the US dollar index), meaning moves in fiber ripple through every dollar-denominated commodity. A strong EUR (rising fiber) tends to support dollar-denominated commodity prices because non-US buyers find commodities cheaper in local currency terms.

## Concrete example
A European oil refiner needs to buy 50 million barrels of crude oil over the next quarter, priced in USD. The total cost is approximately $4 billion. The refiner's revenue is in EUR. To hedge, the refiner sells EUR/USD forward, locking in a rate of 1.0850. If fiber drops to 1.0650 by quarter-end, the forward hedge saves the refiner $200 x 50 million / 1.0650 = approximately $75 million worth of EUR compared to hedging at spot.

The fail scenario: the [[ECB]] cuts rates unexpectedly, fiber drops from 1.0850 to 1.0600 within a week, and the refiner had only hedged 40% of the exposure. The unhedged portion costs an additional EUR 94 million. This shows why commodity firms with EUR-denominated costs must actively manage fiber exposure.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **Quotation convention**: EUR/USD is quoted as USD per 1 EUR. A quote of 1.0850 means 1 EUR = 1.0850 USD.
- **Pip value**: 1 pip = 0.0001. On a 1 million EUR position, 1 pip = $100.
- **Primary trading venues**: [[EBS]] is the dominant [[ECN]] for EUR/USD interbank trading.
- **Typical spread**: 0.1 to 0.3 pips interbank during European/US overlap, widening to 0.5 to 1.5 pips during Asian session.
- **DXY weight**: EUR/USD has the largest weight (57.6%) in the US Dollar Index ([[DXY Correlation]]), making fiber the single most important driver of the broad dollar.
- **Cross-rate relationships**: EUR/GBP = EUR/USD / GBP/USD. EUR/JPY = EUR/USD x USD/JPY. Fiber is the base rate for constructing most EUR crosses.

## Related concepts (learn next)
- [[Cable]]: the slang for GBP/USD, fiber's closest peer among major pairs.
- [[DXY Correlation]]: the US dollar index where EUR/USD has the dominant 57.6% weight, linking fiber to broad dollar strength.
- [[ECB]]: the European Central Bank, whose monetary policy decisions are the primary driver of EUR/USD.
- [[EBS]]: the primary [[ECN]] for interbank EUR/USD trading and price discovery.
- [[FX Swap]]: EUR/USD FX swaps are the most liquid FX derivative instrument globally.
- [[Monetary Policy Divergence]]: the spread between ECB and Fed policy rates is the dominant medium-term driver of fiber.
- [[Carry Trade]]: when there is a wide rate differential between USD and EUR, fiber becomes a carry trade vehicle.
- [[EUR/GBP]]: a cross derived from fiber and cable, used for relative eurozone vs. UK views.

## Common misconceptions
1. **"Fiber is always the most liquid pair, so spreads are always tight"**: While average spreads are the tightest in FX, fiber spreads can blow out during ECB announcements, FOMC decisions, and geopolitical shocks. During the March 2020 COVID crash, EUR/USD spreads widened to 5+ pips even on [[EBS]].
2. **"Fiber only moves on US data"**: EUR/USD is driven by both sides. ECB decisions, Eurozone PMI data, German industrial production, and European political events (elections, debt crises) move fiber as much as US non-farm payrolls or CPI.
3. **"Everyone calls it fiber"**: The term is less universally adopted than "cable." Many desks simply say "euro-dollar" or "EUR/USD." Unlike cable, which has 160 years of history, fiber emerged as slang only after 1999 and is not used by all market participants.
