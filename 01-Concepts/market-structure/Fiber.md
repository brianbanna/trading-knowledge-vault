# Fiber

## Definition
Fiber (or fibre) is the market slang for the EUR/USD [[currency pair]]. The name emerged in the early 2000s when the euro launched, referencing the fiber optic cables that had replaced the telegraph cables of the original "[[Cable]]" (GBP/USD). EUR/USD is the single most traded currency pair globally, roughly 23% of FX turnover. "Fiber is offered at 80" means EUR/USD has sellers at a price ending in 80 (e.g., 1.0880). Common on interbank desks, though less universally adopted than "cable" for GBP/USD.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
EUR/USD is the benchmark FX pair and the most liquid instrument in the world by daily volume (over $700 billion/day). For commodity traders, EUR/USD exposure shows up constantly: European refiners buying USD priced crude, commodity houses in Switzerland or the Netherlands converting USD revenues, cross border metals transactions. EUR/USD is the primary driver of the [[DXY Correlation]] (the US dollar index), so moves in fiber ripple through every dollar denominated commodity. A strong EUR (rising fiber) supports dollar denominated commodity prices because non US buyers find commodities cheaper in local currency.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** A European refiner needs 50 million barrels of crude over the next quarter, priced in USD. Total cost ~$4 billion. Revenue is in EUR. The refiner sells EUR/USD forward at 1.0850 to lock in the rate. Fiber drops to 1.0650 by quarter end; the forward hedge saves $200 × 50,000,000 / 1.0650 = ~$75 million worth of EUR vs spot hedging. Failure mirror: [[ECB]] cuts unexpectedly, fiber drops from 1.0850 to 1.0600 in a week, refiner had only hedged 40%. Unhedged portion costs an extra EUR 94 million.

**Simplified:** Fiber is the nickname for the euro versus the US dollar. It is the most traded pair on the planet with the tightest spreads. Anyone in commodities with euro costs and dollar revenues, or vice versa, has constant fiber exposure and must hedge it. Moves in fiber drive the dollar index, which in turn affects every dollar denominated commodity globally.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **Quotation**: EUR/USD is USD per 1 EUR. 1.0850 means 1 EUR = 1.0850 USD.
- **Pip value**: 0.0001. On EUR 1 million, 1 pip = $100.
- **Primary venues**: [[EBS]] is the dominant [[ECN]] for interbank EUR/USD.
- **Typical spread**: 0.1 to 0.3 pips in Europe/US overlap, 0.5 to 1.5 pips in Asia.
- **DXY weight**: EUR/USD has the largest weight (57.6%) in the US Dollar Index ([[DXY Correlation]]).
- **Cross relationships**: EUR/GBP = EUR/USD / GBP/USD. EUR/JPY = EUR/USD × USD/JPY. Fiber is the base for most EUR crosses.

## Common misconceptions

**"Fiber is always the most liquid pair, so spreads are always tight."** Average spreads are the tightest in FX, but they blow out during ECB announcements, FOMC, and geopolitical shocks. March 2020 COVID crash saw EUR/USD spreads widen to 5+ pips even on [[EBS]].

**"Fiber only moves on US data."** Driven by both sides. ECB decisions, Eurozone PMI, German IP, and European political events move fiber as much as NFP or CPI.

**"Everyone calls it fiber."** Less universal than "cable." Many desks say "euro dollar" or "EUR/USD." Unlike cable's 160 year history, fiber emerged as slang only after 1999.
