# Position

## Definition
A position is your current holding in an instrument. "[[Long]] 10 million EUR/USD" means you own 10 million euros against US dollars. "[[Short]] 500 lots of Brent crude" means you have sold 500 [[Futures Contract]]s and owe delivery or must buy them back. A position has 3 key attributes: instrument, direction ([[Long]] or [[Short]]), and size ([[Notional]] or number of contracts). When you have no position, you are [[Flat]]. Your position is what generates your [[PnL]] as prices move.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
Everything in trading starts and ends with position management. Your position determines your exposure to market moves, your [[Margin]] requirements, and your [[Mark to Market]] [[PnL]]. In FX, a desk might carry positions across dozens of [[Currency Pair]]s simultaneously, and the aggregate book position determines overall risk. In commodities, a physical trader's position includes both futures hedges and the underlying physical inventory. Knowing your exact position at all times is the single most important discipline in trading.

## Concrete example
**Win scenario:** You build a [[Long]] position in copper on the LME, buying 100 lots (2,500 tonnes) at 8,500 USD per tonne. Over 2 weeks, copper rallies to 8,800 USD per tonne. Your position generates a [[PnL]] of 2,500 x (8,800 minus 8,500) = 750,000 USD in [[Unrealized PnL]]. You close 50 lots at 8,800 to lock in 375,000 USD of [[Realized PnL]] and keep 50 lots running.

**Fail scenario:** Instead of rallying, copper drops to 8,200 USD per tonne on China demand fears. Your [[Unrealized PnL]] is 2,500 x (8,200 minus 8,500) = negative 750,000 USD. Your risk manager tells you to cut the position in half. You sell 50 lots at 8,200, turning 375,000 USD of paper loss into [[Realized PnL]]. The remaining 50 lot position continues to show [[Unrealized PnL]] losses.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- Position value = Number of units x Current price
- Position PnL = Number of units x (Current price minus Entry price)
  - For [[Long]]: positive PnL when price rises
  - For [[Short]]: positive PnL when price falls
- Net position = Sum of all [[Long]] minus all [[Short]] in the same instrument
- Gross position = Sum of absolute values of all positions (measures total exposure)

## Related concepts (learn next)
- [[Flat]] , the state of having no position
- [[PnL]] , the profit or loss generated by your position
- [[Mark to Market]] , the daily revaluation of your position
- [[Margin]] , the collateral required to maintain a position
- [[Leverage]] , the ratio between position size and capital
- [[Risk Management]] , the discipline of sizing and hedging positions
- [[Book]] , the aggregate of all positions managed by a trader or desk

## Common misconceptions
- **A position is not a trade.** A trade is an event (buying or selling). A position is a state (what you currently hold). Multiple trades can build, reduce, or flip a position.
- **Net flat does not mean risk free.** You can be net [[Flat]] in EUR/USD but still have a [[Long]] 100 million spot position offset by a [[Short]] 100 million [[Forward Contract]]. The net position is zero, but you still carry [[Basis Risk]] and time risk.
- **Position size and risk are not the same thing.** A 10 million [[Position]] in EUR/USD (daily vol ~0.5%) has far less risk than a 10 million position in USD/TRY (daily vol ~2%).
