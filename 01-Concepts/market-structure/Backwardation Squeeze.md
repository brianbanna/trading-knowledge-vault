# Backwardation Squeeze

## Definition

A backwardation squeeze on the [[LME]] occurs when physical tightness drives the Cash price sharply above the 3 Month forward, forcing shorts who cannot source physical metal to buy back positions at escalating prices. Unlike equity short squeezes (shares can be borrowed), LME squeezes involve physical metal that is genuinely scarce. When [[LME Warehouse Stocks]] are low and [[Cancelled Warrants]] are high, deliverable metal shrinks. Shorts approaching prompt must find metal or roll forward at punitive backwardation. The 2022 LME nickel squeeze (prices spiked from $25,000 to over $100,000/tonne before trades were cancelled) was the most extreme modern example.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Squeezes create the most violent short term moves in metals. A trader caught short faces theoretically unlimited losses because there is no ceiling on backwardation when metal is unavailable. The LME has lending rules (capping any single entity's control over deliverable stocks, mandating lending at capped rates), but these mitigate rather than eliminate the dynamic. Position management means monitoring deliverable stocks, cancellation ratios, and concentration of ownership. For opportunistic traders, identifying pre squeeze conditions (depleted stocks, concentrated short interest) offers asymmetric long opportunities.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** LME copper stocks fall to 25,000 tonnes from 150,000 six months earlier. Cancelled warrants at 60%, effective deliverable copper just 10,000 tonnes. A single trading house holds 40% of outstanding warrants. Cash/3M is at $100 backwardation and widening. A short holder with 5,000 tonnes expiring in 2 days cannot find metal and rolls at $200 backwardation, costing $200/tonne. If the squeeze intensifies to $500 backwardation, the roll costs $500/tonne. A trader long Cash short 3M entered at $50 backwardation and exits at $400, earning $350/tonne.

**Simplified:** Someone sold metal they do not own, expecting to buy it back later. The metal in the warehouse runs out. The shorts must still deliver, so they bid frantically for the few units left. Prices for immediate delivery spike far above prices for delivery a month out. The person sitting on the physical metal collects a fortune from the shorts. The exchange may step in if it gets extreme, but until then the loss for shorts has no ceiling.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Squeeze conditions checklist:**
- LME stocks below 50,000 tonnes (copper)
- Cancellation ratio above 50%
- Single entity controlling > 30% of warrants/positions
- Cash/3M backwardation above $100/tonne (copper)
- Open interest large relative to deliverable stocks

**LME lending rules:** When a single entity holds > 50% of warrants and positions combined, the LME requires them to lend metal at a capped rate (0.5% of LME price per day).

**Theoretical maximum loss for shorts:** Unlimited; no cap on backwardation when metal is unavailable.

## Common misconceptions

**"The LME prevents squeezes."** Lending rules mitigate but do not eliminate. The 2022 nickel event showed rules can be overwhelmed.

**"You can always roll a short."** During a squeeze, the roll cost (backwardation) can effectively force closure at a massive loss.

**"Squeezes only happen in small markets."** The 2022 nickel squeeze involved billions in losses on a core LME contract. Copper has experienced squeezes.
