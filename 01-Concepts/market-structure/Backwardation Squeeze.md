# Backwardation Squeeze

## Definition

A backwardation squeeze occurs on the [[LME]] when physical tightness drives the Cash price sharply above the 3 Month forward, forcing short position holders who cannot source physical metal for delivery to buy back their positions at escalating prices. Unlike equity short squeezes (where shares can be borrowed), LME squeezes involve physical metal that is genuinely scarce. When [[LME Warehouse Stocks]] fall to low levels and [[Cancelled Warrants]] are high, the pool of deliverable metal shrinks. Shorts who are approaching their prompt (delivery) date must either find metal or roll their position forward at increasingly punitive backwardation levels. The 2022 LME nickel squeeze (which saw prices spike from $25,000 to over $100,000/tonne before trades were cancelled) was the most extreme modern example.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Squeezes create the most violent short term price moves in metals markets. A trader caught short during a squeeze faces theoretically unlimited losses because there is no ceiling on backwardation when physical metal is unavailable. The LME has implemented lending rules (limiting any single entity's control over deliverable stocks and requiring lending at capped rates) to mitigate squeeze risk, but these measures have not eliminated the dynamic. For position management, understanding squeeze risk means monitoring deliverable stocks, cancellation ratios, and concentration of ownership. For opportunistic traders, identifying pre squeeze conditions (depleted stocks, concentrated short interest) offers asymmetric long opportunities.

## Concrete example

LME copper stocks fall to 25,000 tonnes (from 150,000 six months ago). Cancelled warrants are at 60%. Effective deliverable copper is only 10,000 tonnes. A single trading house holds 40% of outstanding warrants. The Cash/3M spread is at $100 backwardation and widening daily. A short holder with 5,000 tonnes expiring in 2 days cannot find metal. She is forced to roll at $200 backwardation, costing $200/tonne. If the squeeze intensifies to $500 backwardation, the cost of rolling becomes $500/tonne. A trader who was long Cash/short 3M entered at $50 backwardation and exits at $400, earning $350/tonne.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Squeeze conditions checklist:**
- LME stocks below 50,000 tonnes (for copper)
- Cancellation ratio above 50%
- Single entity controlling > 30% of warrants/positions
- Cash/3M backwardation exceeding $100/tonne (copper)
- Open interest large relative to deliverable stocks

**LME lending rules:** When a single entity holds a dominant position (> 50% of warrants and positions combined), the LME requires them to lend metal at a capped rate (0.5% of LME price per day).

**Theoretical maximum loss for shorts:** Unlimited, because there is no cap on backwardation if metal is genuinely unavailable.

## Common misconceptions

**"The LME prevents squeezes."** The LME has lending rules, but they only mitigate, not eliminate squeeze dynamics. The 2022 nickel event showed that rules can be overwhelmed.

**"You can always roll a short position."** During a squeeze, the cost of rolling (the backwardation) can be so extreme that it effectively forces the short to close at a massive loss.

**"Squeezes only happen in small markets."** The 2022 nickel squeeze involved billions of dollars in losses on one of the LME's core contracts. Copper, the most liquid LME contract, has also experienced squeezes.
