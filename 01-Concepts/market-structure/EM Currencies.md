# EM Currencies

## Definition
Emerging market (EM) currencies are currencies of developing economies that offer higher interest rates, wider [[Bid-Ask Spread|spreads]], lower turnover, and greater political and policy risk than [[G10 Currencies|G10]]. The most actively traded include BRL (Brazilian real), MXN (Mexican peso), ZAR (South African rand), TRY (Turkish lira), INR (Indian rupee), CNH/CNY (Chinese yuan), KRW (South Korean won), PLN (Polish zloty), CZK (Czech koruna), HUF (Hungarian forint). Many EM currencies are subject to capital controls, tradeable only via [[NDF|non deliverable forwards]] rather than spot settlement. EM FX is a distinct asset class with return drivers that differ materially from G10.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
Many EM economies are major commodity producers: Brazil (soybeans, iron ore, coffee), South Africa (gold, platinum), Russia (oil, gas), Chile (copper), Indonesia (palm oil, nickel). Their currencies co move with commodity prices, making EM FX a leveraged play on commodity cycles. [[Carry Trade|Carry]] in EM can be 5 to 15 percentage points above USD rates, far above [[G10 Currencies|G10]] carry. The premium compensates for real risks: sudden devaluations, [[Central Bank Intervention|interventions]], capital controls, political instability. For commodity firms with EM supply chains, unhedged EM exposure can dominate PnL.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** 2021, a carry trader goes long BRL/USD (short USD/BRL) at 5.50, attracted by Brazil's Selic at 13.75% vs Fed funds at 0.25%, an annualized carry of ~13.5%. Over 6 months BRL strengthens to 4.90, earning both carry and spot appreciation, total return over 20% annualized. Failure mirror: 2022 TRY. Despite Turkish rates above 15%, USD/TRY moved from 13 to 18 in a year as Erdogan forced unconventional rate cuts. Carry was overwhelmed by 30%+ depreciation, devastating TRY longs.

**Simplified:** EM currencies pay much higher interest rates than developed market currencies. That extra yield looks attractive, but it compensates for real risks: governments can intervene, capital controls can trap your money, and big devaluations happen periodically. Some EM currencies track commodity prices closely because the country exports raw materials. The high carry works most years, then occasionally blows up and erases years of gains in weeks.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **EM carry** = EM rate − USD rate. BRL rate 13.75%, USD 5.25%: carry = 8.50% annualized.
- **EM spread**: 3 to 10 pips for liquid EM (MXN) to 50+ pips for illiquid (NGN, EGP).
- **NDF settlement**: PnL = (NDF rate − [[Spot Rate|spot]] fixing) × notional / spot fixing. USD cash settled.
- **Sharpe ratio trap**: EM carry shows attractive Sharpe (1.0+) that collapses during tail events; returns are negatively skewed.
- **Commodity beta**: ZAR ~0.6 with gold; BRL ~0.5 with iron ore; MXN ~0.3 with crude.

## Common misconceptions

**High carry means high returns.** Excess yield compensates for crash risk. TRY, ARS, and NGN have all offered double digit carry while delivering catastrophic depreciation.

**EM is a single trade.** Heterogeneous. MXN trades like near G10; NGN is effectively illiquid with a managed peg. One basket ignores critical differences.

**EM risk is only about rates.** Political risk, sanctions, capital controls, and institutional quality often matter more than the rate itself. RUB's 2022 collapse was geopolitics, not monetary policy.
