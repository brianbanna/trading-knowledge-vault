# EM Currencies

## Definition
Emerging market (EM) currencies are the currencies of developing economies that typically offer higher interest rates, wider [[Bid-Ask Spread|bid ask spreads]], lower daily turnover, and greater political and policy risk than [[G10 Currencies|G10 currencies]]. The most actively traded EM currencies include BRL (Brazilian real), MXN (Mexican peso), ZAR (South African rand), TRY (Turkish lira), INR (Indian rupee), CNH/CNY (Chinese yuan), KRW (South Korean won), PLN (Polish zloty), CZK (Czech koruna), and HUF (Hungarian forint). Many EM currencies are subject to capital controls, making them tradeable only via [[NDF|non deliverable forwards]] rather than spot settlement. EM FX represents a distinct asset class with return drivers that differ materially from G10.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
Many EM economies are major commodity producers: Brazil (soybeans, iron ore, coffee), South Africa (gold, platinum), Russia (oil, gas), Chile (copper), and Indonesia (palm oil, nickel). Their currencies therefore co move with commodity prices, making EM FX a leveraged play on commodity cycles. The [[Carry Trade|carry]] available in EM currencies can be 5 to 15 percentage points above USD rates, far exceeding [[G10 Currencies|G10]] carry. However, this premium compensates for real risks: sudden devaluations, [[Central Bank Intervention|central bank interventions]], capital controls, and political instability. For commodity firms with EM supply chains, unhedged EM currency exposure can dominate P&L.

## Concrete example
In 2021, a carry trader goes long BRL/USD (short USD/BRL) at 5.50, attracted by Brazil's Selic rate of 13.75% versus the US Fed funds rate of 0.25%, generating an annualized carry of roughly 13.5%. Over 6 months, BRL strengthens to 4.90, and the trader earns both carry and spot appreciation, a total return exceeding 20% annualized. However, in 2022, TRY provided a cautionary tale: despite Turkish rates above 15%, USD/TRY moved from 13 to 18 in a year as President Erdogan forced unconventional rate cuts. The carry was overwhelmed by an over 30% depreciation, producing devastating losses for TRY longs.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **EM carry** = EM interest rate − USD interest rate. If BRL rate = 13.75% and USD rate = 5.25%, carry = 8.50% annualized.
- **EM spread**: Typical bid ask spreads range from 3 to 10 pips for liquid EM (MXN) to 50+ pips for illiquid EM (NGN, EGP).
- **NDF settlement**: P&L = (NDF rate − [[Spot Rate|spot]] fixing rate) × notional / spot fixing rate. All cash settled in USD.
- **Sharpe ratio trap**: EM carry strategies often show attractive Sharpe ratios (1.0+) that collapse during tail events because returns are negatively skewed.
- **Commodity beta**: ZAR correlates ~0.6 with gold; BRL correlates ~0.5 with iron ore; MXN correlates ~0.3 with crude oil.

## Related concepts (learn next)
- [[NDF]]: the primary instrument for trading EM currencies with capital controls (CNH, INR, KRW).
- [[Carry Trade]]: EM currencies are the high yield leg of carry strategies, offering the richest but riskiest premiums.
- [[Central Bank Intervention]]: EM central banks actively intervene to defend pegs or manage volatility, creating discontinuous price action.
- [[Carry Unwind]]: EM carry positions unwind violently during risk off episodes, with correlations spiking to 1.
- [[Terms of Trade]]: commodity price shocks directly affect EM exporters' currencies through trade balance channels.
- [[Cross Currency Basis]]: EM basis can be extremely wide, reflecting dollar funding stress in developing economies.
- [[Petrocurrency]]: overlap with EM includes BRL, RUB, and MXN as oil linked EM currencies.

## Common misconceptions
1. **High carry means high returns**: The excess yield in EM currencies compensates for crash risk. TRY, ARS, and NGN have all offered double digit carry while delivering catastrophic spot depreciation.
2. **EM is a single trade**: EM currencies are heterogeneous. MXN trades like a liquid, near G10 currency; NGN is effectively illiquid with a managed peg. Treating them as 1 basket ignores critical differences.
3. **EM risk is only about rates**: Political risk, sanctions, capital controls, and institutional quality often matter more than the interest rate itself. Russia's RUB collapse in 2022 was driven by geopolitics, not monetary policy.
