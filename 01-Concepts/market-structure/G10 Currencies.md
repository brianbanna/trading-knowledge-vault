# G10 Currencies

## Definition
The G10 currencies are the 10 most heavily traded and liquid currencies in the world: USD, EUR, JPY, GBP, CHF, AUD, CAD, NZD, NOK, and SEK. Despite the name, this group does not correspond to the G10 group of nations; it is an FX market convention that emerged from interbank trading desks. These currencies share key characteristics: free floating exchange rates (or managed floats), deep and liquid spot and derivatives markets, minimal capital controls, and backing by central banks with credible monetary policy frameworks. Together, the 45 possible G10 [[Currency Pair|currency pairs]] account for the vast majority of daily global FX turnover, estimated at over 80% of the roughly 7.5 trillion USD daily market.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
G10 currencies are the default universe for most systematic FX strategies including [[Carry Trade|carry]], momentum, and value. Transaction costs are lowest in G10 pairs, making them suitable for higher frequency and leveraged strategies. Several G10 currencies have strong commodity linkages: AUD (iron ore, coal), CAD (crude oil), NOK (North Sea crude), and NZD (dairy, agriculture). This makes G10 FX a natural overlay for commodity traders managing currency exposure or expressing commodity views through currency proxies.

## Concrete example
A macro fund constructs a G10 carry portfolio: long AUD, NZD, and NOK (higher yielding) versus short CHF, JPY, and EUR (lower yielding). When the Reserve Bank of Australia holds rates at 4.35% while the Bank of Japan remains at 0.10%, AUD/JPY carry is approximately 4.25% annualized. The portfolio earns positive carry in calm markets. However, during August 2024's volatility spike, JPY surged as [[Carry Unwind|carry trades unwound]], and AUD/JPY fell from 105 to 94 in days, wiping out months of carry income. The lesson: G10 carry is accessible and liquid but still vulnerable to sharp reversals.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **The 10 currencies**: USD, EUR, JPY, GBP, CHF, AUD, CAD, NZD, NOK, SEK.
- **Number of unique pairs**: 10 × 9 / 2 = 45 pairs.
- **Liquidity hierarchy** (by daily volume): EUR/USD > USD/JPY > GBP/USD > AUD/USD > USD/CAD > USD/CHF > NZD/USD > EUR/GBP > others.
- **Commodity linked G10**: AUD (iron ore, coal), CAD (crude oil, natural gas), NOK (Brent crude), NZD (dairy, soft commodities).
- **Funding currencies**: JPY, CHF, EUR (low or negative rates historically).

## Related concepts (learn next)
- [[Major Pairs]]: the subset of G10 pairs that include USD, representing the highest volume and tightest spreads.
- [[Cross Pairs]]: G10 pairs excluding USD, useful for isolating bilateral themes like EUR vs GBP.
- [[EM Currencies]]: the complement to G10, offering higher carry but with wider spreads and political risk.
- [[Dollar Index]]: a weighted index measuring USD against 6 of the G10 currencies.
- [[Carry Trade]]: a strategy that exploits [[Interest Rate Differential|rate differentials]] across G10 and beyond.
- [[Petrocurrency]]: commodity linked G10 members (NOK, CAD) whose exchange rates co move with oil.
- [[Monetary Policy Divergence]]: the primary driver of G10 FX trends, as central banks move rates at different paces.

## Common misconceptions
1. **G10 currencies are risk free**: G10 currencies can experience extreme moves. GBP fell over 8% on the night of the Brexit referendum in June 2016, and CHF surged 30% intraday when the SNB removed its EUR/CHF floor in January 2015.
2. **G10 means the G10 nations**: The FX G10 list does not match any official political grouping. Norway and Sweden are included in FX G10 but not in, say, the G7 or the political G10.
3. **All G10 pairs are equally liquid**: EUR/USD trades roughly 2 trillion USD daily, while NOK/SEK might trade a few billion. Liquidity varies enormously within the G10 universe.
