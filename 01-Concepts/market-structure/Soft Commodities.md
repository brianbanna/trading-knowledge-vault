# Soft Commodities

## Definition

Soft commodities are agricultural products that are grown (not mined or extracted). The category includes [[Coffee Futures|coffee]], [[Cocoa Futures|cocoa]], sugar, cotton, orange juice, and sometimes grains and oilseeds, though the narrower definition restricts "softs" to tropical/subtropical crops. The key distinguishing features: production is weather dependent, supply is seasonal and geographically concentrated (often in developing countries), and the products are perishable or have finite storage life. This contrasts with [[Hard Commodities]] (metals, energy), which are extracted, have more stable production profiles, and can be stored indefinitely.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Soft commodities offer some of the most volatile and trend driven moves in the commodity complex because supply is concentrated in a few producing countries and cannot be ramped up quickly (a cocoa tree takes 3 to 5 years to produce, coffee 2 to 3 years). A drought in Brazil can remove 20% of global coffee supply in a single season with no quick substitute. This creates [[Supply Shock]] dynamics that are sharper and longer lasting than in energy or metals. For FX traders, soft commodity price moves directly affect the currencies of producing countries: BRL (coffee, sugar, soybeans), COP (coffee), GHS (cocoa), CLP (agricultural exports).

## Concrete example

Cocoa in 2024: West African cocoa production (Ghana and Ivory Coast = ~60% of global supply) collapsed due to disease (swollen shoot virus) and adverse weather. Cocoa futures surged from $2,500/tonne to over $10,000/tonne. Unlike oil (where OPEC can increase production in weeks) or copper (where mines can ramp output), cocoa trees take years to replant. The supply deficit persisted, and prices remained elevated. A trader who understood the biological supply constraint recognized this as a multi-year [[Structural Break]], not a temporary spike for [[Mean Reversion]].

## Key mechanics and formulas

No formulas specific to the category, but key metrics:
- **Stock to use ratio:** `Stocks / Annual Consumption`. Below 20% for most softs signals tightness. Below 10% is a supply crisis.
- **Days of forward cover:** `Ending Stocks / (Annual Consumption / 365)`. Similar concept.
- **Crop year:** Softs trade on crop year calendars, not calendar years. Brazilian coffee crop year is Jul to Jun. Cocoa is Oct to Sep.

## Common misconceptions

**"Softs are niche markets."** Coffee and sugar are among the most traded commodities globally by volume. The ICE Sugar No. 11 contract is one of the most liquid agricultural futures.

**"You can mean revert softs like you mean revert energy spreads."** Soft commodity supply shocks can last multiple crop years because replanting takes years. Mean reversion assumptions are dangerous when biological production cycles are the bottleneck.

**"Softs are only affected by weather."** Government policy (Brazil's ethanol mandate supporting sugar prices, export bans, subsidies), currency moves (BRL weakness makes Brazilian coffee cheaper in USD), and speculative positioning all drive prices.
