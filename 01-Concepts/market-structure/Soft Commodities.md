# Soft Commodities

## Definition

Soft commodities are agricultural products that are grown rather than mined or extracted. The category includes [[Coffee Futures|coffee]], [[Cocoa Futures|cocoa]], sugar, cotton, orange juice, and sometimes grains and oilseeds (though the narrower definition restricts "softs" to tropical/subtropical crops). Distinguishing features: production is weather dependent, supply is seasonal and geographically concentrated (often in developing countries), and the products are perishable or have finite storage life. This contrasts with [[Hard Commodities]] (metals, energy), which are extracted, have stable production profiles, and store indefinitely.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Soft commodities offer some of the most volatile and trend driven moves in the complex because supply concentrates in a few producers and cannot ramp quickly (a cocoa tree takes 3 to 5 years to produce, coffee 2 to 3). A Brazilian drought removes 20% of global coffee in a season with no quick substitute. This produces [[Supply Shock]] dynamics sharper and longer lasting than in energy or metals. For FX traders, soft commodity moves directly affect producer currencies: BRL (coffee, sugar, soybeans), COP (coffee), GHS (cocoa), CLP (agricultural exports).

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** Cocoa 2024: West African production (Ghana + Ivory Coast ≈ 60% of global) collapsed on swollen shoot virus and adverse weather. Cocoa futures surged from $2,500/tonne to over $10,000. Unlike oil (OPEC increases production in weeks) or copper (mines ramp), cocoa trees take years to replant. The deficit persisted and prices stayed elevated. A trader who understood the biological supply constraint recognized this as a multi year [[Structural Break]], not a temporary spike to fade via [[Mean Reversion]]. Position sized accordingly captured the move; positions sized on mean reversion assumptions got destroyed.

**Simplified:** Crops that are grown in specific tropical regions (coffee, cocoa, sugar, cotton). Production has biological lag (years to replant trees). Supply shocks last because you cannot just turn on more output. That makes them more trending and more dangerous to short into a drought or disease cycle than energy or metals.

## Key mechanics and formulas

No formulas specific to the category, but key metrics:
- **Stock to use ratio:** `Stocks / Annual Consumption`. Below 20% for most softs signals tightness. Below 10% is a supply crisis.
- **Days of forward cover:** `Ending Stocks / (Annual Consumption / 365)`. Similar concept.
- **Crop year:** softs trade on crop year calendars, not calendar years. Brazilian coffee crop year is Jul to Jun. Cocoa is Oct to Sep.

- [[Convenience Yield]]: high in softs because of crop failure risk and limited substitutability.

## Common misconceptions

**"Softs are niche markets."** Coffee and sugar are among the most traded commodities globally by volume. ICE Sugar No. 11 is one of the most liquid agricultural futures.

**"You can mean revert softs like energy spreads."** Soft supply shocks last multiple crop years because replanting takes years. Mean reversion assumptions are dangerous when biological cycles are the bottleneck.

**"Softs are only affected by weather."** Government policy (Brazil ethanol mandate supporting sugar, export bans, subsidies), currency moves (BRL weakness makes Brazilian coffee cheaper in USD), and speculative positioning all drive prices.
