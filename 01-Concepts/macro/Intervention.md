# Intervention

## Definition

Intervention is when a central bank enters the FX market to buy or sell its own currency to influence the exchange rate. Direct (actual buying/selling of currency reserves) or verbal (officials making market moving statements). Central banks intervene to combat excessive volatility, defend a peg, prevent disorderly moves, or push back against speculation. Intervention is one of the most powerful and unpredictable forces in FX.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Intervention creates sudden, violent moves that wipe out positions in seconds. A trader short [[USD JPY]] ahead of a BOJ intervention loses months of PnL in minutes. For commodity traders, intervention that sharply moves the dollar ripples through all USD denominated commodities. Knowing which central banks are likely to intervene, at what levels, and in what style is essential for risk management.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** BOJ intervention October 2022. [[USD JPY]] breaks above 150.00 for the first time since 1990. The BOJ intervenes, selling an estimated $42bn of USD reserves over several sessions. USD/JPY slams from 151.90 to 146.20 in hours. A trader long USD/JPY at 148.50 targeting 150.50, knowing BOJ has a line near 150, takes profit at 149.80 and goes [[Flat]]. A momentum trader who stays long through 150 expecting the breakout gets crushed: the 570 pip drop gaps through a [[Stop Loss]] at 149.00 due to [[Gap Risk]], fills at 147.50, loses 440 pips ($44,000 on 10 lots).

**Simplified:** A central bank does not like where its currency is. It dumps reserves to push the rate the other way. If you are positioned with the prevailing trend, you eat a violent reversal. Stops do not protect you because there is no liquidity to fill into. The defense: trim or flatten before known pain levels, and size small if you must hold through.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Types of intervention:**

| Type | Description | Impact |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Unsterilized | CB buys/sells FX, money supply changes | Strongest, affects rates too |
| Sterilized | CB buys/sells FX, offsets with bond operations | Moderate, signal effect only |
| Verbal | Officials talk the currency up or down | Weakest, but effective if credible |
| Coordinated | Multiple CBs act together (Plaza Accord, 2011 G7) | Most powerful |

**Key variables:**
- Reserve size: firepower (Japan: ~$1.2tn, Switzerland: ~$800bn)
- Credibility: will the market believe the CB will follow through?
- Direction: defending weakness (selling reserves) is harder than defending strength (can print own currency)

**Intervention pain level (rough):**
Expected cost = intervention size x market momentum against the CB

## Common misconceptions

1. **"Intervention always works."** Short term, usually yes. Long term, intervention against the fundamental trend almost always fails. The Bank of England's 1992 ERM defense (Black Wednesday) is the textbook example.
2. **"Central banks intervene at announced levels."** Most CBs deliberately avoid stating intervention levels. Ambiguity is a tool. BOJ has never confirmed a specific line.
3. **"Verbal intervention is harmless."** A credible "we are watching closely" or "ready to act decisively" moves markets immediately. Verbal intervention is free and buys time.
4. **"You can predict when intervention will happen."** You can identify likely zones (past behavior, pain thresholds, political pressure), but exact timing is unknowable. Position sizing near intervention levels must be conservative.
