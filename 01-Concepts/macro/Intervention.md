# Intervention

## Definition

Intervention is when a central bank enters the foreign exchange market to buy or sell its own currency, aiming to influence the exchange rate. It can be direct (actual buying/selling of currency reserves) or verbal (officials making statements designed to move the market). Central banks intervene to combat excessive volatility, defend a peg, prevent disorderly moves, or push back against speculation. Intervention is one of the most powerful and unpredictable forces in FX markets.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Intervention creates sudden, violent price moves that can wipe out positions in seconds. An FX trader short [[USD JPY]] ahead of a Bank of Japan intervention can lose months of PnL in minutes. For commodity traders, intervention that sharply strengthens or weakens the dollar ripples through all USD denominated commodities. Knowing which central banks are likely to intervene, at what levels, and in what style is essential for risk management.

## Concrete example

**BOJ intervention (October 2022):** [[USD JPY]] breaks above 150.00 for the first time since 1990. The Bank of Japan intervenes, selling an estimated $42 billion of USD reserves over several sessions.

**Success case (for the aware trader):** A trader is long USD/JPY at 148.50 with a 150.50 target. Knowing the BOJ has drawn a line near 150, the trader takes profit at 149.80 and goes [[Flat]]. When intervention slams the pair from 151.90 to 146.20 in hours, the trader is safely out.

**Failure case:** A momentum trader stays long through 150, expecting the breakout to continue. The intervention triggers a 570 pip crash. The [[Stop Loss]] at 149.00 gets gapped through due to [[Gap Risk]], and the trader is filled at 147.50, losing 440 pips ($44,000 on a 10 lot position).

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Types of intervention:**

| Type | Description | Impact |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Unsterilized | CB buys/sells FX, money supply changes | Strongest, affects rates too |
| Sterilized | CB buys/sells FX, offsets with bond operations | Moderate, signal effect only |
| Verbal | Officials talk the currency up or down | Weakest, but can be effective if credible |
| Coordinated | Multiple CBs act together (Plaza Accord, 2011 G7) | Most powerful |

**Key variables:**
- Reserve size: how much firepower the CB has (Japan: ~$1.2 trillion, Switzerland: ~$800 billion)
- Credibility: does the market believe the CB will follow through?
- Direction: defending weakness (selling reserves) is harder than defending strength (can print own currency)

**No formula per se, but the intervention "pain level" is roughly:**
Expected cost = intervention size * (market momentum against the CB)

## Common misconceptions

1. **"Intervention always works."** Short term, yes, it usually moves the price. Long term, intervention against the fundamental trend almost always fails. The Bank of England's 1992 ERM defense (Black Wednesday) is the textbook example.

2. **"Central banks intervene at announced levels."** Most central banks deliberately avoid stating intervention levels. Ambiguity is a tool. The BOJ has never confirmed a specific line for USD/JPY.

3. **"Verbal intervention is harmless."** When a credible central banker says "we are watching closely" or "ready to act decisively," markets move immediately. Verbal intervention is free and can buy time.

4. **"You can predict when intervention will happen."** You can identify likely zones (based on past behavior, pain thresholds, political pressure), but the exact timing is unknowable. This is why [[Position]] sizing near intervention levels must be conservative.
