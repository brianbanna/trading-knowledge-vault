# Chokepoint

## Definition

A chokepoint is a narrow maritime passage through which significant volumes of oil and other commodities must transit. The most critical oil chokepoints: Strait of Hormuz (~20 mb/d, Persian Gulf to Indian Ocean), Strait of Malacca (~16 mb/d, Indian Ocean to Pacific), Suez Canal and SUMED Pipeline (~5 mb/d, Red Sea to Mediterranean), and Bab el Mandeb (~5 mb/d, southern Red Sea). The Turkish Straits (Bosporus and Dardanelles, ~3 mb/d) and Panama Canal also carry significant volumes. Disruption at these points affects global oil supply routes.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Chokepoint risk is the single largest source of geopolitical risk premium in oil. A closure at the Strait of Hormuz would remove roughly 20% of global oil supply, dwarfing any OPEC cut in history. Even temporary disruptions create spikes: Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping in 2024 forced rerouting around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10 to 14 days of transit and $2 to $4/bbl in [[Freight]] costs. The risk premium is highest when [[Spare Capacity]] is low, because the market has less buffer. For FX, chokepoint events strengthen [[Petrocurrency]] pairs and weaken oil importer currencies.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** January 2024, Houthi forces begin attacking commercial shipping in Bab el Mandeb. Tanker operators reroute around the Cape of Good Hope. Brent rises $5/bbl in 2 weeks on the disruption. VLCC freight rates from the Middle East to Europe double. A trader long Brent call options and long tanker freight futures profits on both legs. If attacks had resolved via ceasefire, Brent gives back the $5 premium and the long loses. If conflict escalated to close Hormuz, Brent could spike $20 to $40.

**Simplified:** Most of the world's oil moves through 4 or 5 narrow straits. Tankers cannot reroute quickly: each detour costs days at sea, fuel, and crew. When a chokepoint gets threatened, traders price in the worst case before it happens. Oil rises, tanker rates rise, and currencies of exporters strengthen against importers. If the threat fizzles, the premium fades. If a chokepoint actually closes, the move dwarfs anything OPEC could engineer.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Major chokepoints and volumes:**
| Chokepoint | Volume (mb/d) | Key Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Strait of Hormuz | ~20 | Iran military threats, regional conflict |
| Strait of Malacca | ~16 | Piracy, geopolitical tensions |
| Suez Canal / SUMED | ~5 | Regional conflict, blockage (Ever Given 2021) |
| Bab el Mandeb | ~5 | Yemen conflict, Houthi attacks |
| Turkish Straits | ~3 | Russia/Ukraine conflict, regulatory changes |
| Panama Canal | ~1 | Drought reducing capacity |

**Rerouting cost:** Cape of Good Hope route adds 10 to 14 days and $2 to $4/bbl vs Suez for Middle East to Europe.

## Common misconceptions

**"Hormuz has never been closed, so it never will be."** Iran has threatened closure multiple times and the market prices the risk. Partial disruption (mining a few ships) would cause massive spikes.

**"Rerouting is a minor inconvenience."** During the 2024 Red Sea disruptions, the effective global tanker fleet shrank by ~10% because vessels spent more time in transit.

**"Chokepoint risk is always priced in."** Markets underestimate tail risks. Sudden escalations create sharp repricing. The actual disruption price spike exceeds the prior premium.
