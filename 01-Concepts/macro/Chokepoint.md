# Chokepoint

## Definition

A chokepoint is a narrow maritime passage through which significant volumes of oil and other commodities must transit. The most critical oil chokepoints are the Strait of Hormuz (~20 million barrels per day, connecting the Persian Gulf to the Indian Ocean), the Strait of Malacca (~16 million barrels per day, linking the Indian Ocean to the Pacific), the Suez Canal and SUMED Pipeline (~5 million barrels per day, connecting the Red Sea to the Mediterranean), and Bab el Mandeb (~5 million barrels per day, at the southern entrance to the Red Sea). The Turkish Straits (Bosporus and Dardanelles, ~3 million barrels per day) and the Panama Canal also carry significant volumes. Any disruption at these points affects global oil supply routes.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Chokepoint risk is the single largest source of geopolitical risk premium in oil prices. A closure or sustained disruption at the Strait of Hormuz would remove roughly 20% of global oil supply from the market, dwarfing any OPEC cut in history. Even temporary disruptions create price spikes: Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping in 2024 forced tankers to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10 to 14 days of transit time and $2 to $4/bbl in [[Freight]] costs. The chokepoint risk premium is highest when [[Spare Capacity]] is low, because the market has less buffer to absorb any disruption. For FX traders, chokepoint events strengthen [[Petrocurrency]] pairs and weaken currencies of oil importing nations.

## Concrete example

In January 2024, Houthi forces begin attacking commercial shipping in Bab el Mandeb. Tanker operators reroute around the Cape of Good Hope. Brent crude rises $5/bbl in 2 weeks on the disruption risk. VLCC freight rates from the Middle East to Europe double. A trader who was long Brent call options and long tanker freight futures profits on both legs. If the attacks had been resolved quickly through a ceasefire, Brent would have given back the $5 premium and the long position would have lost. If the conflict had escalated to close Hormuz, Brent could have spiked $20 to $40.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Major chokepoints and volumes:**
| Chokepoint | Volume (mb/d) | Key Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Strait of Hormuz | ~20 | Iran military threats, regional conflict |
| Strait of Malacca | ~16 | Piracy, geopolitical tensions |
| Suez Canal / SUMED | ~5 | Regional conflict, blockage (Ever Given 2021) |
| Bab el Mandeb | ~5 | Yemen conflict, Houthi attacks |
| Turkish Straits | ~3 | Russia/Ukraine conflict, regulatory changes |
| Panama Canal | ~1 | Drought reducing capacity |

**Rerouting cost:** Cape of Good Hope route adds approximately 10 to 14 days and $2 to $4/bbl vs Suez for Middle East to Europe.

## Common misconceptions

**"Hormuz has never been closed, so it never will be."** While Hormuz has not been fully closed, Iran has threatened closure multiple times, and the market prices the risk. Even partial disruption (mining a few ships) would cause massive price spikes.

**"Rerouting is a minor inconvenience."** Rerouting adds significant costs and time. During the 2024 Red Sea disruptions, the effective global tanker fleet shrank by approximately 10% because vessels spent more time in transit.

**"Chokepoint risk is always priced in."** The market tends to underestimate tail risks. Sudden escalations create sharp repricing events. The risk premium is highest before an event, but the price spike from an actual disruption typically exceeds the prior premium.
