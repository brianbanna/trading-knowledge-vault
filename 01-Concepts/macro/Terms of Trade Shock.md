# Terms of Trade Shock

## Definition
A terms of trade (ToT) shock is a sudden, significant change in the ratio of a country's export prices to its import prices, driven by a sharp move in commodity prices, trade policy, or supply chain disruption. For commodity exporters (Australia, Canada, Norway, Brazil, Chile), a surge in their primary export (iron ore, oil, copper) improves ToT, boosting national income and strengthening the currency. For importers (Japan, India, Turkey, most of Europe), the same spike worsens ToT, draining income and weakening the currency. ToT shocks produce some of the most powerful and persistent FX trends because they directly alter the current account, corporate profitability, and fiscal position. The FX response is delayed and incomplete, creating multi month opportunities for systematic and macro strategies.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
ToT shocks are the primary fundamental driver of commodity linked FX pairs. When crude spikes from $70 to $120, Norway's ToT improves (oil is 40 to 50% of exports), strengthening NOK. Japan's ToT deteriorates (Japan imports virtually all energy), weakening JPY. NOKJPY has a powerful positive beta to oil. For systematic traders, ToT driven commodity FX relationships are among the most reliable cross asset signals: AUDUSD tracks iron ore and copper, USDCAD inversely tracks WTI, NZDUSD tracks dairy. These persist because they are grounded in real economic flows (trade balances, corporate hedging, sovereign wealth fund repatriation), not sentiment. Understanding ToT is essential for designing cross asset carry, momentum, and value strategies bridging FX and commodities.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** February 2022, Russia's invasion of Ukraine triggers a massive oil supply shock. Brent rallies $90 to $130 in 2 weeks. Positive ToT shock: USDNOK falls 9.10 to 8.60 (NOK +5.5%), USDCAD falls 1.28 to 1.24 (CAD +3%), USDBRL falls 5.20 to 4.80 (BRL +7.7%). Negative ToT shock: USDJPY rises 115 to 122 (JPY -6%), USDINR rises 75 to 77.5 (INR -3.3%), EURUSD falls 1.13 to 1.08 (EUR weakens as Europe's gas costs skyrocket). A macro trader who went long NOK, CAD, BRL and short JPY, INR, EUR captured 5 to 8% returns in 3 weeks. By June 2022, oil began declining from $120; traders who did not reduce ToT positions suffered reversals. Lesson: ToT shocks create powerful but finite impulses; the trade must be monitored as the commodity itself evolves.

**Simplified:** When a key commodity price moves, it creates winners and losers based on whether a country sells it or buys it. Exporters get richer in real terms and their currency strengthens. Importers get poorer and their currency weakens. Trade the spread: long the winner, short the loser. The signal lasts months because the trade balance adjusts slowly. When the commodity reverses, the trade reverses too.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **Terms of trade**: ToT = export price index / import price index; increase = improvement = positive for currency
- **ToT change**: Delta ToT = (change in export prices x export share) - (change in import prices x import share)
- **Commodity beta of a currency**: Beta = Cov(currency return, commodity return) / Var(commodity return); AUDUSD beta to iron ore ≈ 0.15, USDCAD beta to WTI ≈ -0.25, NOKJPY beta to Brent ≈ 0.20 to 0.30
- **Current account impact**: CA change = Delta ToT x trade openness (trade as % of GDP); larger openness amplifies FX effect
- **FX fair value shift**: Delta(FX fair value) = k x Delta(ToT), where k depends on trade structure and elasticities
- **Key commodity FX pairs**:
  - AUD: iron ore, coal, copper
  - CAD: crude oil, natural gas
  - NOK: crude oil, natural gas
  - NZD: dairy, meat, forestry
  - BRL: soybeans, iron ore, crude oil
  - CLP: copper
  - ZAR: gold, platinum, coal

## Related concepts (learn next)
- [[DXY Correlation]]: ToT shocks in USD terms are amplified or dampened by the broader dollar trend.
- [[Carry Cross Asset]]: commodity exporters often have higher interest rates when prices are high, creating reinforcing signals.
- [[Momentum Cross Asset]]: ToT driven FX trends are persistent and well captured by momentum systems.
- [[Macro Regime]]: major ToT shocks can trigger regime transitions (an oil shock causing Stagflation in importers).
- [[Real Rates Regime]]: central banks in importers may need to hike real rates to defend their currency after a negative ToT shock.
- [[Correlation Breakdown]]: during a massive ToT shock, usual commodity FX correlations intensify rather than break, creating unusually strong signals.
- [[Value Cross Asset]]: PPP based value for commodity currencies must account for ToT shifts; an improvement can justify a permanently higher exchange rate.
- [[Positioning Signal]]: extreme speculative positioning after a ToT shock signals potential mean reversion.

## Common misconceptions
1. **"Commodity currencies only move because of commodity prices"**: ToT is dominant, but commodity currencies also respond to domestic factors (CB policy, political risk, housing). AUD is sensitive to Chinese growth expectations and Australian mortgage rates independently of iron ore.
2. **"ToT shocks are always temporary"**: Some are structural (shale oil permanently improved US energy ToT; the energy transition may permanently improve lithium and copper exporters' ToT). Assuming mean reversion in all cases is dangerous.
3. **"The FX response to a ToT shock is immediate"**: Empirical research shows currencies take 3 to 12 months to fully adjust, creating a delayed reaction systematic strategies capture. Initial move = 30 to 50% of eventual adjustment.
4. **"All commodity exporters react the same way"**: Each country's export basket matters. An oil shock helps Canada and Norway, does nothing for Chile (copper). A copper shock helps Chile and Australia, not Norway.
