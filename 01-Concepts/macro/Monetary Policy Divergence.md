# Monetary Policy Divergence

## Definition
Monetary policy divergence occurs when central banks of different countries move rates or policy stances in opposite directions or at very different speeds. It is the strongest and most persistent driver of exchange rate trends, because widening [[Interest Rate Differential|interest rate differentials]] attract capital toward the higher yielding currency while raising the funding cost for positions in the lower yielding currency. Divergence can be 1 CB hiking while another holds, 1 hiking while another cuts, or both moving the same direction at very different paces. The FX market is forward looking: what matters is not current policy rates but the expected path priced by overnight index swaps (OIS) and rate futures.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
Policy divergence has driven every major FX trend of the past decade. The 2014 to 2016 dollar rally was fueled by Fed tightening while ECB and BOJ eased. The 2022 dollar surge to 20 year highs came as the Fed hiked aggressively while the BOJ held at zero. For commodities, a strong USD (driven by hawkish Fed divergence) pressures dollar denominated commodity prices, tightening global financial conditions. [[Petrocurrency|Petrocurrency]] CBs sometimes respond to Fed hikes with their own tightening, amplifying the divergence theme into EM FX.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** In 2022, the Fed raised rates from 0.25% to 4.50% while the BOJ held yield curve control near 0%. The US/Japan policy rate gap widened from 0.35% to 4.50%. USD/JPY rose from 115 to 152, over 3,200 [[Pip|pips]] and 32%. [[Dollar Index|DXY]] surged from 96 to 114. A macro trader long USD/JPY at 120 in March 2022 and held to 150 in October captured 3,000 pips. In late 2023 and 2024, the divergence narrative reversed as the market priced Fed cuts while BOJ signaled normalization. USD/JPY fell from 158 to 140 in a violent [[Carry Unwind|carry unwind]], punishing traders who assumed divergence was permanent.

**Simplified:** Two central banks moving in opposite directions create a yield gap that pulls money from the low yielder into the high yielder. The currency of the high yielder rises. That move can run for years. When the gap stops widening, or starts to narrow, the trade reverses violently because everyone is positioned the same way. The trade is not about today's rate but about where rates are heading.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **Rate differential trajectory**: The change in IRD (Δ IRD) matters more than the level. An IRD moving 2% to 4% drives a stronger trend than a static 4% IRD.
- **OIS spread**: The 2 year OIS spread between 2 countries is the most common market measure of expected policy divergence.
- **Rule of thumb**: A 100 bps shift in the 2 year US/Europe OIS spread moves EUR/USD ~5 to 8 big figures (500 to 800 pips), varies by regime.
- **Taylor rule differential**: Some models estimate "fair value" FX by comparing Taylor rule implied policy rates across countries.
- **Convergence signal**: When divergence peaks and begins to narrow (Fed signals pause while ECB still hiking), the FX trend reverses, often violently.

## Related concepts (learn next)
- [[Interest Rate Differential]]: direct output of policy divergence, driving [[Forward Points|forward points]] and carry.
- [[Carry Trade]]: divergence creates the rate differentials that make carry trades profitable.
- [[Dollar Index]]: DXY trends are overwhelmingly driven by Fed policy relative to ECB and BOJ (EUR and JPY weights).
- [[Central Bank Intervention]]: when divergence driven FX moves become disorderly, CBs may intervene to slow the trend.
- [[Carry Unwind]]: reversal of divergence expectations triggers carry unwinds as the market reprices the rate path.
- [[Real Effective Exchange Rate]]: sustained divergence pushes currencies far from [[Real Effective Exchange Rate|REER]] equilibrium, creating long run reversion risk.
- [[Uncovered Interest Parity]]: UIP predicts divergence should not produce excess FX returns; its failure is why divergence trades work.
- [[Purchasing Power Parity]]: extreme divergence driven misalignment eventually corrects toward PPP, but "eventually" can mean years.

## Common misconceptions
1. **Divergence is just about rate levels**: The market is forward looking. If the Fed is at 5% but expected to cut to 3%, while the ECB is at 3% but expected to hold, divergence is narrowing even though the Fed rate is still higher. Paths matter more than spots.
2. **Divergence trades are low risk**: These trends persist for years, tempting overconfidence and excessive leverage. When the narrative shifts, reversals are 2 to 3 times faster than the original trend as carry positions unwind.
3. **All central banks respond to the same data**: Different mandates (Fed dual; ECB price stability; BOJ inflation expectations). The same global shock produces divergent policy responses, which creates the opportunity.
