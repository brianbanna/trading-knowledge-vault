# Monetary Policy Divergence

## Definition
Monetary policy divergence occurs when central banks of different countries move their interest rates or policy stances in opposite directions or at significantly different speeds. It is the strongest and most persistent driver of exchange rate trends, because widening [[Interest Rate Differential|interest rate differentials]] attract capital flows toward the higher yielding currency while simultaneously increasing the cost of funding positions in the lower yielding currency. Divergence can manifest as 1 central bank hiking while another holds, 1 hiking while another cuts, or both moving in the same direction but at very different paces. The FX market is forward looking: what matters most is not current policy rates but the expected path of future rates as priced by overnight index swaps (OIS) and rate futures.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
Policy divergence has driven every major FX trend of the past decade. The 2014 to 2016 dollar rally was fueled by Fed tightening while the ECB and BOJ eased. The 2022 dollar surge to 20 year highs occurred as the Fed hiked aggressively while the BOJ held at zero. For commodity markets, divergence matters because a strong USD (driven by hawkish Fed divergence) exerts downward pressure on dollar denominated commodity prices, tightening financial conditions globally. [[Petrocurrency|Petrocurrency]] central banks sometimes respond to Fed hikes with their own tightening, amplifying the divergence theme into EM FX.

## Concrete example
In 2022, the Fed raised rates from 0.25% to 4.50% while the Bank of Japan maintained its yield curve control policy at approximately 0%. The US/Japan policy rate gap widened from 0.35% to 4.50%. USD/JPY rose from 115 to 152, a move of over 3,200 [[Pip|pips]] and roughly 32%. The [[Dollar Index|DXY]] surged from 96 to 114. A macro trader who went long USD/JPY at 120 in March 2022 and held to 150 in October captured 3,000 pips. However, in late 2023 and 2024, the divergence narrative reversed as the market began pricing Fed cuts while the BOJ signaled normalization. USD/JPY fell from 158 to 140 in a violent [[Carry Unwind|carry unwind]], punishing traders who assumed divergence was permanent.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **Rate differential trajectory**: The change in the rate differential (Δ IRD) is more important for spot FX than the level of the IRD. An IRD moving from 2% to 4% drives a stronger trend than a static 4% IRD.
- **OIS spread**: The difference in 2 year OIS rates between 2 countries is the most commonly used market measure of expected policy divergence.
- **Rule of thumb**: A 100 bps shift in the 2 year US/Europe OIS spread moves EUR/USD approximately 5 to 8 big figures (500 to 800 pips), though this varies by regime.
- **Taylor rule differential**: Some models estimate "fair value" FX rates by comparing Taylor rule implied policy rates across countries.
- **Convergence signal**: When divergence peaks and begins to narrow (e.g., Fed signals a pause while ECB is still hiking), the FX trend reverses, often violently.

## Related concepts (learn next)
- [[Interest Rate Differential]]: the direct output of policy divergence, driving [[Forward Points|forward points]] and carry.
- [[Carry Trade]]: divergence creates the rate differentials that make carry trades profitable.
- [[Dollar Index]]: DXY trends are overwhelmingly driven by Fed policy relative to ECB and BOJ policy (due to EUR and JPY weights).
- [[Central Bank Intervention]]: when divergence driven FX moves become "disorderly," central banks may intervene to slow the trend.
- [[Carry Unwind]]: the reversal of divergence expectations triggers carry unwinds as the market re prices the rate path.
- [[Real Effective Exchange Rate]]: sustained divergence can push currencies far from [[Real Effective Exchange Rate|REER]] equilibrium, creating long run reversion risk.
- [[Uncovered Interest Parity]]: UIP predicts divergence should not produce excess FX returns; its failure is why divergence trades work.
- [[Purchasing Power Parity]]: extreme divergence driven misalignment eventually corrects toward PPP, but "eventually" can mean years.

## Common misconceptions
1. **Divergence is just about rate levels**: The market is forward looking. If the Fed is at 5% but expected to cut to 3%, while the ECB is at 3% but expected to hold, the divergence is narrowing even though the Fed rate is still higher. Expected paths matter more than spot rates.
2. **Divergence trades are low risk**: These trends can persist for years, tempting overconfidence and excessive leverage. When the narrative shifts, the reversal is typically 2 to 3 times faster than the original trend, as carry positions unwind.
3. **All central banks respond to the same data**: Central banks have different mandates (the Fed has dual mandate; the ECB focuses on price stability; the BOJ targets inflation expectations). The same global shock can produce divergent policy responses, which is exactly what creates the opportunity.
