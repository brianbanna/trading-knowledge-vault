# Green Metals

## Definition

Green metals is a collective term for the metals critical to the global energy transition: [[Copper Futures|copper]] (electrical wiring, EV motors, grid infrastructure), [[Nickel Futures|nickel]] (EV battery cathodes), lithium (battery anodes and cathodes), cobalt (battery cathodes), rare earth elements (permanent magnets for wind turbines and EV motors), and to a lesser extent [[Aluminum Futures|aluminum]] (lightweighting, solar frames) and [[Zinc Futures|zinc]] (wind turbine galvanizing). The energy transition requires massive quantities of these metals: an electric vehicle uses 2 to 4 times more copper than an internal combustion vehicle, and a single offshore wind turbine uses over 8 tonnes of copper. The IEA estimates that clean energy mineral demand must roughly quadruple by 2040 to meet net zero targets.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Green metals represent one of the most significant structural demand shifts in commodity markets in decades. Unlike cyclical demand (which rises and falls with GDP), energy transition demand is policy driven and directionally persistent. This creates a new demand baseline on top of traditional industrial demand. However, supply responses are slow: new copper mines take 10 to 15 years from discovery to production. This structural supply/demand imbalance underpins the long term bull case for green metals. For traders, the challenge is timing: the fundamental story is strong but price overshoots and corrections are common. EV adoption rates, government subsidy policies, and technology shifts (LFP vs NMC battery chemistry) create volatile intermediate term dynamics.

## Concrete example

A trader builds a long term bullish copper position based on the energy transition thesis. Copper is at $9,000/tonne. Global copper mine supply growth is projected at 2% per year while EV and grid demand adds 3% per year. She goes long December 2028 copper at $9,500 (deferred contracts are in contango). If the energy transition accelerates and supply disappoints, copper could reach $12,000 by 2028, earning $2,500/tonne. If a recession delays EV adoption and Indonesian/DRC mine supply ramps faster than expected, copper could stagnate at $8,500, losing $1,000/tonne on the deferred position plus contango roll costs.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Metal intensity per unit:**
- EV: 53 to 83 kg copper, 40 kg nickel (NMC), 8 to 12 kg lithium, 5 to 15 kg cobalt
- ICE vehicle: 23 kg copper
- Offshore wind turbine (8 MW): 8,000 kg copper
- Solar panel (per MW): 2,500 to 5,000 kg copper

**IEA mineral demand projections (net zero scenario):**
- Copper: ~2x current demand by 2040
- Nickel: ~3x
- Lithium: ~7x
- Cobalt: ~3.5x

## Common misconceptions

**"The green metals supercycle is guaranteed."** Technology substitution (replacing cobalt with LFP batteries, aluminum wiring replacing copper) can reduce metal intensity. The demand trajectory depends on which technologies win.

**"Supply cannot respond."** Indonesian nickel supply has already crashed nickel prices despite strong EV demand. Supply responses can be faster than expected for some metals.

**"All green metals will benefit equally."** Cobalt demand is being engineered out of many battery chemistries. Lithium faces potential oversupply from Latin American brine projects. Each metal has its own dynamics.
