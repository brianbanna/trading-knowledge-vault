# Green Metals

## Definition

Green metals is a collective term for metals critical to the global energy transition: [[Copper Futures|copper]] (wiring, EV motors, grid), [[Nickel Futures|nickel]] (EV battery cathodes), lithium (battery anodes and cathodes), cobalt (battery cathodes), rare earth elements (permanent magnets for wind turbines and EV motors), and to a lesser degree [[Aluminum Futures|aluminum]] (lightweighting, solar frames) and [[Zinc Futures|zinc]] (wind turbine galvanizing). An EV uses 2 to 4 times more copper than an ICE vehicle, and an offshore wind turbine uses 8+ tonnes of copper. The IEA estimates clean energy mineral demand must roughly quadruple by 2040 to meet net zero targets.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Green metals represent one of the most significant structural demand shifts in commodity markets in decades. Unlike cyclical demand (rises and falls with GDP), energy transition demand is policy driven and directionally persistent. This adds a new demand baseline on top of traditional industrial demand. Supply responses are slow: new copper mines take 10 to 15 years from discovery to production. The structural imbalance underpins the long term bull case. Timing is hard: the fundamental story is strong but overshoots and corrections are common. EV adoption rates, subsidy policies, and chemistry shifts (LFP vs NMC) create volatile intermediate dynamics.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** A trader builds a long term bullish copper position on the energy transition thesis. Copper at $9,000/tonne. Global mine supply growth projected at 2% per year while EV and grid demand adds 3% per year. She goes long December 2028 copper at $9,500 (deferred in contango). If transition accelerates and supply disappoints, copper could reach $12,000 by 2028, earning $2,500/tonne. If recession delays EV adoption and Indonesian/DRC supply ramps faster than expected, copper could stagnate at $8,500, losing $1,000/tonne on the deferred position plus contango roll costs.

**Simplified:** The world is electrifying. Wires, motors, batteries, and turbines all need metals. Demand is rising for structural reasons that do not care about the GDP cycle. New mines take a decade or more. That gap between fast demand and slow supply pushes prices up over time. Short term, prices still bounce on China data, recession fears, and tech substitution. Long term, the math is hard to escape unless a substitute appears.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Metal intensity per unit:**
- EV: 53 to 83 kg copper, 40 kg nickel (NMC), 8 to 12 kg lithium, 5 to 15 kg cobalt
- ICE vehicle: 23 kg copper
- Offshore wind turbine (8 MW): 8,000 kg copper
- Solar panel (per MW): 2,500 to 5,000 kg copper

**IEA mineral demand projections (net zero scenario):**
- Copper: ~2x current demand by 2040
- Nickel: ~3x
- Lithium: ~7x
- Cobalt: ~3.5x

## Common misconceptions

**"The supercycle is guaranteed."** Technology substitution (LFP replacing cobalt, aluminum wiring replacing copper) can reduce metal intensity. The trajectory depends on which technologies win.

**"Supply cannot respond."** Indonesian nickel supply crashed nickel prices despite strong EV demand. Supply responses can be faster than expected for some metals.

**"All green metals benefit equally."** Cobalt is being engineered out of many battery chemistries. Lithium faces potential oversupply from Latin American brine projects. Each metal has its own dynamics.
