# Petrocurrency

## Definition
A petrocurrency is a currency whose value is significantly linked to the price of crude oil, either because the country is a major oil exporter or because oil revenues constitute a dominant share of its economy. The most commonly cited petrocurrencies are NOK (Norwegian krone), CAD (Canadian dollar), RUB (Russian ruble), BRL (Brazilian real), and MXN (Mexican peso). The linkage operates through the trade balance: when oil prices rise, oil exporting nations earn more foreign currency, improving their current account, boosting demand for the domestic currency, and typically driving appreciation. The strength of the correlation varies by country and period but generally ranges from 0.3 to 0.7 on a rolling 1 year basis for the most oil dependent economies.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
For commodity traders, petrocurrencies offer a way to express oil views through FX markets, which are often more liquid and offer better leverage than physical oil contracts. A trader bullish on Brent crude can buy NOK/SEK as a proxy, isolating the oil exposure while avoiding energy specific risks like contango or storage. Conversely, oil producers with revenues in USD but costs in CAD or NOK have natural petrocurrency exposure that must be hedged. Understanding the petrocurrency dynamic is essential for portfolio construction: a portfolio long crude oil and long CAD has concentrated, correlated risk that may appear diversified but is not.

## Concrete example
In 2022, Brent crude surged from 78 to 128 USD/barrel following Russia's invasion of Ukraine. NOK/USD appreciated from 0.113 to 0.104 (NOK strengthened roughly 8%), and USD/CAD fell from 1.28 to 1.22 (CAD strengthened roughly 5%). A trader who bought 10 million NOK/SEK at 0.9800 when oil broke above 100 rode the pair to 1.0150, a gain of 350 [[Pip|pips]]. However, in late 2023 when oil fell from 95 to 72 despite OPEC+ cuts, NOK weakened sharply: EUR/NOK rose from 11.20 to 11.80, a 5.4% NOK depreciation. A long NOK position lost money despite OPEC+ supply discipline, because demand fears dominated.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **Oil beta**: The regression coefficient of currency returns on oil price returns. NOK beta to Brent ≈ 0.15 to 0.25 (a 10% oil rally corresponds to 1.5 to 2.5% NOK appreciation, roughly).
- **Oil share of GDP**: Norway ~15%, Russia ~15 to 20%, Saudi Arabia ~40%+. Higher share = stronger petrocurrency link.
- **Hedging ratio**: If a Canadian oil producer earns 100 million USD/year and costs are in CAD, the FX hedge ratio depends on the oil beta of CAD and the production hedge ratio.
- **Rolling correlation**: Petrocurrency correlation to oil varies. CAD/oil correlation has ranged from 0.2 to 0.7 over rolling 2 year windows since 2000.
- **Sovereign wealth fund channel**: Norway's Government Pension Fund Global reinvests oil revenues abroad, dampening the NOK appreciation that would otherwise occur from oil windfalls.

## Related concepts (learn next)
- [[Terms of Trade]]: the mechanism through which oil prices transmit to petrocurrency values via export/import price ratios.
- [[Cross Pairs]]: NOK/SEK and AUD/CAD are cross pairs that isolate oil vs non oil commodity dynamics.
- [[Dollar Index]]: CAD is a DXY component, so oil rallies that strengthen CAD also weigh on DXY.
- [[Monetary Policy Divergence]]: petrocurrency central banks sometimes raise rates in response to oil driven inflation, amplifying the currency move.
- [[Central Bank Intervention]]: EM petrocurrency central banks (e.g., Russia, Brazil) actively intervene to smooth oil driven volatility.
- [[Carry Trade]]: some petrocurrencies (BRL, MXN) also offer high carry, creating a dual oil + carry exposure.
- [[Real Effective Exchange Rate]]: petrocurrencies can become overvalued on REER during sustained oil booms ("Dutch disease").

## Common misconceptions
1. **All petrocurrencies move in lockstep with oil**: The relationship is statistical, not deterministic. CAD is also driven by US economic data and the Fed, NOK by European gas prices and Norges Bank policy, and BRL by domestic politics and Selic rate decisions.
2. **Petrocurrencies only move on WTI or Brent**: The relevant benchmark varies. CAD tracks WCS (Western Canadian Select) and WTI; NOK tracks Brent and European natural gas; RUB tracked Urals crude before sanctions disrupted the relationship.
3. **Oil importers are anti petrocurrencies**: While JPY and INR tend to weaken when oil rises (as import costs increase), the correlation is weaker and less consistent than for exporters because other macro factors dominate.
