# Petrocurrency

## Definition
A petrocurrency is a currency whose value is significantly linked to crude oil, either because the country is a major oil exporter or because oil revenues dominate its economy. Most cited petrocurrencies: NOK, CAD, RUB, BRL, MXN. The linkage runs through the trade balance: when oil rises, oil exporters earn more foreign currency, improving their current account, raising demand for domestic currency, driving appreciation. Correlation varies by country and period but ranges from 0.3 to 0.7 on a rolling 1 year basis for the most oil dependent economies.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
For commodity traders, petrocurrencies allow expressing oil views through FX, often more liquid with better leverage than physical oil contracts. A trader bullish Brent can buy NOK/SEK as a proxy, isolating oil exposure while avoiding contango or storage. Oil producers with USD revenues but CAD or NOK costs have natural petrocurrency exposure that must be hedged. Understanding the petrocurrency dynamic is essential for portfolio construction: long crude and long CAD has concentrated, correlated risk that looks diversified but is not.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** In 2022, Brent surged from 78 to 128 USD/barrel after Russia's invasion of Ukraine. NOK/USD appreciated from 0.113 to 0.104 (NOK +8%), USD/CAD fell from 1.28 to 1.22 (CAD +5%). A trader who bought 10mm NOK/SEK at 0.9800 when oil broke 100 rode the pair to 1.0150, +350 [[Pip|pips]]. In late 2023 when oil fell from 95 to 72 despite OPEC+ cuts, NOK weakened: EUR/NOK rose 11.20 to 11.80, a 5.4% depreciation. The long NOK position lost despite supply discipline because demand fears dominated.

**Simplified:** When a country sells a lot of oil, an oil rally floods its trade balance with dollars. Those dollars get exchanged for the local currency, which then strengthens. Trading the currency is often easier than trading the oil futures themselves (more liquid, no roll). The catch: the currency also responds to its own central bank, politics, and global risk, so the oil signal can get drowned out for weeks or months.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **Oil beta**: Regression coefficient of currency returns on oil returns. NOK beta to Brent ≈ 0.15 to 0.25 (10% oil rally ≈ 1.5 to 2.5% NOK appreciation).
- **Oil share of GDP**: Norway ~15%, Russia ~15 to 20%, Saudi Arabia ~40%+. Higher share = stronger petrocurrency link.
- **Hedging ratio**: If a Canadian oil producer earns $100mm/year and costs are in CAD, the FX hedge ratio depends on the oil beta of CAD and the production hedge ratio.
- **Rolling correlation**: CAD/oil correlation has ranged from 0.2 to 0.7 over rolling 2 year windows since 2000.
- **Sovereign wealth fund channel**: Norway's Government Pension Fund Global reinvests oil revenues abroad, dampening NOK appreciation that would otherwise occur from oil windfalls.

## Related concepts (learn next)
- [[Terms of Trade]]: the mechanism through which oil prices transmit to petrocurrency values via export/import price ratios.
- [[Cross Pairs]]: NOK/SEK and AUD/CAD isolate oil vs non oil commodity dynamics.
- [[Dollar Index]]: CAD is a DXY component, so oil rallies that strengthen CAD also weigh on DXY.
- [[Monetary Policy Divergence]]: petrocurrency CBs sometimes raise rates in response to oil driven inflation, amplifying the currency move.
- [[Central Bank Intervention]]: EM petrocurrency CBs (Russia, Brazil) actively intervene to smooth oil driven volatility.
- [[Carry Trade]]: some petrocurrencies (BRL, MXN) also offer high carry, creating dual oil + carry exposure.
- [[Real Effective Exchange Rate]]: petrocurrencies can become overvalued on REER during sustained oil booms (Dutch disease).

## Common misconceptions
1. **All petrocurrencies move in lockstep with oil**: Statistical, not deterministic. CAD also driven by US data and the Fed, NOK by European gas and Norges Bank, BRL by domestic politics and Selic.
2. **Petrocurrencies only move on WTI or Brent**: CAD tracks WCS and WTI; NOK tracks Brent and European natural gas; RUB tracked Urals before sanctions disrupted the relationship.
3. **Oil importers are anti petrocurrencies**: JPY and INR weaken when oil rises (import costs), but correlation is weaker and less consistent than for exporters because other macro factors dominate.
