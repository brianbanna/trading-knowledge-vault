# Central Bank Policy

## Definition

Central bank policy refers to the set of tools and frameworks used by central banks (Fed, ECB, BOJ, BOE, PBOC, and others) to manage monetary conditions in their economies. The primary tools are setting short term interest rates, conducting open market operations (QE/QT), forward guidance, and in some cases direct intervention in currency or bond markets. Central bank policy is the single most important macro variable for financial markets because it determines the cost of money, which flows into every asset price.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Central bank rate decisions are the dominant driver of FX markets through the [[Carry Trade]] channel: rate differentials between countries drive capital flows and exchange rates. For commodities, monetary policy affects prices through 3 channels: the USD channel (tighter Fed policy strengthens USD, pressuring commodity prices), the demand channel (higher rates slow economic activity and commodity demand), and the financing channel (higher rates increase the [[Cost of Carry Model|cost of carry]] for holding commodity inventory, discouraging stockpiling).

Every major commodity and FX move in the past 20 years has had a central bank policy component. You cannot trade macro without understanding this.

## Concrete example

The Fed raised rates from 0.25% to 5.50% between March 2022 and July 2023. This simultaneously: strengthened USD (DXY rose from 98 to 114 peak), crushed [[Gold Futures|gold]] from $2,050 to $1,620 as real yields surged, widened the US rate advantage over Europe and Japan driving EUR/USD from 1.12 to 0.96 and USD/JPY from 115 to 152, and slowed global industrial demand which pressured [[Copper Futures|copper]] and base metals.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Taylor Rule (simplified):**
`i = r* + pi + 0.5(pi - pi*) + 0.5(y - y*)`

Where: i = target rate, r* = neutral real rate, pi = inflation, pi* = target inflation, y = output, y* = potential output.

This is the framework central banks loosely follow (though none rigidly). When inflation exceeds target or output exceeds potential, rates go up.

**Real interest rate:**
`Real rate = Nominal rate - Inflation expectations`

Real rates are what actually matter for asset prices, not nominal rates.

**Balance sheet mechanics (QE/QT):**
QE: central bank buys bonds, creates reserves, increases balance sheet, suppresses long term yields, weakens currency.
QT: central bank lets bonds mature or sells, destroys reserves, shrinks balance sheet, allows yields to rise, strengthens currency.

## Related concepts (learn next)

- [[Forward Guidance]] - how central banks signal future policy direction. Markets react to the expected path of rates, not just current rates. Understanding the dot plot, projections, and communication strategy is critical.
- [[Yield Curve]] - the shape of the yield curve reflects market expectations of future central bank policy. Inverted curves signal expected rate cuts (recession expectations).
- [[Quantitative Easing and Tightening]] - balance sheet policy. QE/QT affects long duration assets differently than rate policy affects short duration assets.
- [[Carry Trade]] - rate differentials from central bank policy divergence are the direct driver of FX carry returns.
- [[Dollar Smile]] - the framework for understanding when USD strengthens (US outperformance OR global crisis) and when it weakens (global synchronized growth). Central bank policy drives both scenarios.
- [[FOMC Meeting Playbook]] - tactical trading around Fed meetings. Understanding statement language, dot plot mechanics, and press conference dynamics.
- [[ECB Policy Mechanics]] - different mandate, different tools, different communication style than the Fed. Important for EUR/USD trading.

## Common misconceptions

**"Rate hikes always strengthen the currency."** Only if the hikes are more hawkish than market expectations. A 25bp hike when the market expected 50bp is effectively dovish and can weaken the currency.

**"Central banks control long term rates."** They directly control the overnight rate. Long term rates are set by the market based on growth expectations, inflation expectations, term premium, and supply/demand for bonds. QE/QT influences this but does not control it.

**"You can trade the rate decision itself."** The decision is usually priced in via fed funds futures. The edge is in trading the unexpected: the statement language, the dot plot revisions, the press conference tone. The decision itself is typically a non event.

**"All central banks follow the Fed."** Many EM central banks are forced to follow the Fed to defend their currencies, but major central banks (ECB, BOJ, BOE) can and do diverge from the Fed for extended periods. This divergence is what creates FX carry opportunities.
