# Central Bank Policy

## Definition

Central bank policy is the set of tools and frameworks central banks (Fed, ECB, BOJ, BOE, PBOC) use to manage monetary conditions. Primary tools: setting short term interest rates, open market operations (QE/QT), forward guidance, and in some cases direct intervention in currency or bond markets. Central bank policy is the dominant macro variable for financial markets because it sets the cost of money, which flows into every asset price.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Central bank rate decisions drive FX through the [[Carry Trade]] channel: rate differentials between countries drive capital flows and exchange rates. For commodities, monetary policy hits prices through 3 channels: the USD channel (tighter Fed strengthens USD, pressuring commodity prices), the demand channel (higher rates slow activity and commodity demand), and the financing channel (higher rates raise the [[Cost of Carry Model|cost of carry]] for inventory, discouraging stockpiling).

Every major commodity and FX move in the past 20 years has a central bank policy component. You cannot trade macro without it.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** The Fed raised rates from 0.25% to 5.50% between March 2022 and July 2023. Simultaneously: USD strengthened (DXY 98 to 114 peak), [[Gold Futures|gold]] dropped from $2,050 to $1,620 as real yields surged, the US rate advantage over Europe and Japan widened, driving EUR/USD from 1.12 to 0.96 and USD/JPY from 115 to 152, and global industrial demand slowed, pressuring [[Copper Futures|copper]] and base metals.

**Simplified:** When a central bank raises rates faster than other countries, money flows into that country's currency to earn the yield. The currency strengthens. Commodities priced in that currency get more expensive for foreigners, so demand softens and prices fall. Borrowing costs rise, slowing the real economy, which cuts industrial demand. All of these run at once. The Fed cycle drives everything because USD is the global pricing unit.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Taylor Rule (simplified):**
`i = r* + pi + 0.5(pi - pi*) + 0.5(y - y*)`

Where: i = target rate, r* = neutral real rate, pi = inflation, pi* = target inflation, y = output, y* = potential output.

The framework central banks loosely follow. When inflation exceeds target or output exceeds potential, rates rise.

**Real interest rate:**
`Real rate = Nominal rate - Inflation expectations`

Real rates are what matter for asset prices, not nominal rates.

**Balance sheet mechanics (QE/QT):**
QE: CB buys bonds, creates reserves, expands balance sheet, suppresses long term yields, weakens currency.
QT: CB lets bonds mature or sells, destroys reserves, shrinks balance sheet, allows yields to rise, strengthens currency.

## Related concepts (learn next)

- [[Forward Guidance]] - how CBs signal future policy. Markets react to the expected path of rates, not just current rates. Understand the dot plot, projections, communication strategy.
- [[Yield Curve]] - shape reflects market expectations of future policy. Inverted curves signal expected cuts.
- [[Quantitative Easing and Tightening]] - balance sheet policy. QE/QT affects long duration assets differently than rate policy affects short duration.
- [[Carry Trade]] - rate differentials from policy divergence drive FX carry returns directly.
- [[Dollar Smile]] - framework for when USD strengthens (US outperformance OR global crisis) vs weakens (synchronized global growth).
- [[FOMC Meeting Playbook]] - tactical trading around Fed meetings. Statement language, dot plot, press conference dynamics.
- [[ECB Policy Mechanics]] - different mandate, tools, communication style than the Fed. Important for EUR/USD trading.

## Common misconceptions

**"Rate hikes always strengthen the currency."** Only if more hawkish than market expectations. A 25bp hike when the market expected 50bp is effectively dovish and weakens the currency.

**"Central banks control long term rates."** They control the overnight rate directly. Long term rates are set by markets through growth expectations, inflation expectations, term premium, and bond supply/demand. QE/QT influences but does not control.

**"You can trade the rate decision itself."** The decision is priced in via fed funds futures. Edge lies in trading the unexpected: statement language, dot plot revisions, press conference tone. The decision itself is a non event.

**"All central banks follow the Fed."** Many EM CBs are forced to follow to defend their currencies, but major CBs (ECB, BOJ, BOE) diverge from the Fed for extended periods. This divergence creates FX carry opportunities.
