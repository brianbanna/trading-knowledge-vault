# Central Bank Buying

## Definition

Central bank buying refers to the accumulation of gold reserves by sovereign central banks and monetary authorities. Since 2022, central banks globally have been purchasing gold at a pace exceeding 1,000 tonnes per year, a dramatic structural shift from the prior two decades when central banks were net sellers. The largest buyers have been China (PBOC), India (RBI), Turkey, Poland, Czech Republic, and Singapore. Central bank purchases are motivated by reserve diversification away from the US dollar, geopolitical risk hedging (particularly after the freezing of Russian reserves in 2022), and the desire for an asset with no counterparty risk.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)

Central bank buying has become the largest single source of gold demand, surpassing jewelry, ETFs, and industrial use. At over 1,000 tonnes per year (roughly 25% of annual mine production), official sector purchases provide a structural demand floor for gold prices. This buying is largely price insensitive (central banks buy on dips and at all time highs), making it fundamentally different from speculative demand. For gold traders, central bank buying explains why gold has remained resilient even during periods of rising [[Real Interest Rates]] and strong USD (which historically were bearish). For FX traders, central bank gold accumulation is part of the broader de-dollarization trend, affecting USD reserve currency dynamics.

## Concrete example

The PBOC reports gold reserve additions of 30 tonnes per month for 18 consecutive months in 2023 to 2024. Gold rallies from $1,900 to $2,400 during this period despite rising US real rates and a strong dollar. Traditional models (gold = inverse real rates) would have predicted gold to fall. A trader who tracked central bank buying as a structural demand shift went long gold and was rewarded. If the PBOC had suddenly stopped buying (as it occasionally pauses), the market might have corrected $100 to $200 as the demand floor perception weakened.

## Key mechanics and formulas

**Annual central bank purchases (2022 to 2025):** 1,000 to 1,200 tonnes per year.

**Key buyers and estimated annual purchases:**
- China (PBOC): 200 to 300 tonnes (reported + unreported)
- India (RBI): 50 to 100 tonnes
- Turkey: 50 to 100 tonnes
- Poland: 80 to 130 tonnes
- Other (Czech Republic, Singapore, etc.): 100 to 200 tonnes

**As share of market:** Central bank buying ≈ 25% of annual mine production (~3,600 tonnes).

**Data sources:** World Gold Council quarterly central bank survey, IMF International Financial Statistics (reported reserves).

## Common misconceptions

**"Central bank buying is transparent."** Many central banks (especially China) underreport purchases. Actual buying is likely higher than official IMF data suggests.

**"Central bank buying will stop when gold gets too expensive."** Central banks are price insensitive strategic buyers. They purchased at $1,900, $2,100, and $2,400 without hesitation.

**"This is temporary."** The motivation (reserve diversification, geopolitical hedging) is structural, not cyclical. Barring a major geopolitical de-escalation, the trend is likely to persist.
