# Real Rates Regime

## Definition
The real rates regime is the prevailing level and direction of real (inflation adjusted) interest rates, a structural driver of asset allocation and cross asset pricing. Real rate = nominal rate minus expected inflation (TIPS breakevens, inflation swaps, or surveys). When real rates are deeply negative (nominal below inflation), the opportunity cost of holding non yielding assets like gold and commodities is low, and financial repression pushes capital into inflation hedges. When real rates are significantly positive, cash and bonds offer attractive real returns, drawing capital away from commodities, gold, and speculative risk. The real rate regime is distinct from (though related to) the broader [[Macro Regime]] because it captures central bank stance relative to inflation. Real rates are the most important single variable connecting monetary policy, FX, and commodities.

## Why it matters (commodities and FX)
Real rates are the dominant driver of gold over 6 to 24 month horizons, with -0.7 to -0.9 correlation between US 10 year TIPS yields and gold. Mechanism: at -2% real rates, gold (zero yield) costs nothing in real terms while cash loses purchasing power. At +2%, gold's zero yield is a disadvantage. From 2020 to 2022, deeply negative US real rates (-1.5% on 10 year TIPS) drove gold above $2,000 and fueled a broad commodity bull market. The regime drives FX: higher real rate countries attract capital (real rate adjusted carry), and USD strengthens when US real rates rise vs peers. For [[Risk Parity]] portfolios, the regime determines bond allocation returns (the largest notional) and outsized impact on total performance.

## Concrete example

**Concrete:** August 2020, US 10 year TIPS yield falls to -1.08%, lowest in history. Fed funds near 0%, CPI 1.3% but rising, Fed signals extended accommodation. Gold surges from $1,500 to $2,075 (+38% in 5 months), silver doubles, copper rallies 60%. AUDUSD rises 0.66 to 0.78 as weak USD/strong commodity currency regime takes hold. A macro trader monitoring real rates goes long gold, AUDUSD, copper, capturing the bulk. By October 2022, the regime fully reversed: Fed at 4%, TIPS yields +1.7% (280bp swing), gold at $1,620, AUDUSD at 0.63. A trader who flipped from long to short commodities when real rates crossed zero captured both legs. A trader who ignored real rates and stayed long gold through 2022 suffered a 22% drawdown before recovery.

**Simplified:** Take the bond yield, subtract expected inflation. That number is the real cost of money. When it is very negative, the system is rigged against savers, so people buy hard assets and gold. When it goes positive, savers get paid to wait, so they sell hard assets. Track this number and you have a single dial that predicts most of gold and a lot of commodity FX.

## Key mechanics and formulas
- **Real rate**: Real rate = nominal yield - expected inflation; market based = TIPS yield directly
- **Breakeven inflation**: Breakeven = nominal yield - TIPS yield; measures market inflation expectation
- **Gold real rate regression**: Gold price change = alpha + beta x (change in 10yr TIPS yield) + error; beta ≈ -$150 to -$200 per 100bp change
- **Real rate differential (FX)**: RRD = (real rate A) - (real rate B); positive RRD attracts capital to A
- **Commodity real rate sensitivity**: gold highest (beta ≈ -0.8), silver (-0.6), industrial metals (-0.3)
- **Financial repression threshold**: when real rates < -1%, incentive to hold real assets (commodities, real estate, equities) becomes very strong; historically coincides with commodity bull markets

## Related concepts (learn next)
- [[DXY Correlation]]: rising real rates strengthen USD, which weakens commodities through the dollar channel, creating a compounding effect.
- [[Macro Regime]]: the real rate environment is a sub component; Goldilocks features moderate positive real rates, Stagflation features negative with high inflation.
- [[Carry Cross Asset]]: real rate adjusted carry (rather than nominal) is a more robust signal for FX and bonds.
- [[Risk Parity]]: the bond allocation is a direct bet on the real rate regime; rising real rates erode bond returns.
- [[Value Cross Asset]]: real rates inform fair value of currencies and commodities; negative real rates justify higher commodity valuations.
- [[Momentum Cross Asset]]: real rate trends are slow moving and produce persistent trends in gold, bonds, FX that momentum captures.
- [[Terms of Trade Shock]]: commodity price shocks driven by real rate shifts feed through to terms of trade for exporters.

## Common misconceptions
1. **"Nominal rates drive gold, not real rates"**: Gold is insensitive to nominal moves accompanied by proportional inflation changes. A hike from 2% to 4% with inflation rising 2% to 4% leaves real rates unchanged and barely moves gold. The change in real rates matters.
2. **"Negative real rates are abnormal"**: They occurred for extended periods in the 1940s, 1970s, and 2010 to 2022. They are a policy choice (financial repression) to erode government debt. Treating them as temporary leads to premature positioning.
3. **"TIPS yields perfectly measure real rates"**: TIPS incorporate a liquidity premium (less liquid than nominal Treasuries) and risk premium, so they diverge from "true" real rates. In March 2020, TIPS yields spiked higher even as real economic rates fell.
